# Skool's New Traffic Sources Feature: See Exactly Where Your Members Come From

Skool News #45 covers a genuinely useful platform update: a new Growth dashboard that tells community owners where their visitors, signups, and revenue are actually coming from, plus a per-member "joined from" tag. The episode walks through the dashboard itself, how the tracking works behind the scenes, and where the feature is gated by plan.

## What the Growth dashboard actually shows

Inside a community's admin settings, a new "Growth" tab opens a panel titled "Let's grow your community!" with four headline numbers for a chosen date range (the demo used the last 30 days): total visitors, signups, conversion rate, and new MRR. Below that sits a donut chart breaking down where those visitors and signups came from, plus a line chart of visits and signups over time.

The demo community in the video pulled 17,912 visitors and 736 signups in 30 days, a 4.1% conversion rate, and $12,427 in new MRR. Traffic sources are the same sourced categories on both charts: Skool network, Facebook, Direct, Instagram, Affiliate, Google, YouTube, and Other, each shown with a raw count and percentage. In one screen the top signup source was the Skool network itself at 45.2%, with Facebook a distant second.

## How the tracking actually works, and the one gotcha

The mechanism is deliberately simple: you share one Skool link, people land on your about page and sign up, and Skool shows you where they came from. There is no UTM tagging or per-channel link to manage.

The one thing worth flagging for anyone setting this up is how "Direct" gets calculated. A tooltip in the dashboard (shown on screen in the episode) explains it plainly: "Direct is typically: people visiting the link directly, email broadcasts, or a redirect link/URL shortener." The practical implication is that link-in-bio tools, redirects, and URL shorteners strip the referrer data before it reaches Skool, so that traffic gets miscounted as Direct even when it actually came from Instagram or TikTok. Skool's own recommendation is to link your about page URL directly wherever tracking accuracy matters, rather than routing it through a shortener.

Attribution is last-touch, the window currently only covers the trailing 30 days (the hosts said more historical range is coming), and ads and organic traffic are currently lumped into the same source buckets, with separating them out flagged as a near-term roadmap item.

## The members list gets the same treatment

Beyond the aggregate dashboard, the community's member list now tags each individual member with how they joined, for example "Joined from Facebook," "Joined from YouTube," "Joined from Skool network," or "Joined directly." That turns an anonymous signup count into something you can actually act on: cross-reference your highest-LTV members against their source and double down on the channel that's actually producing them, rather than just the one with the most raw signups.

## Pro vs. Hobby: this is a paid-plan feature

This is not available equally across Skool's pricing tiers. Communities on the Pro plan get the full breakdown across all tracked sources. Communities on the Hobby plan only get the Skool network figure broken out; every other source is blurred out in the dashboard. If growth is the process, that visibility gap alone is a real argument for anyone serious about growth to be on Pro rather than Hobby.

## Why this matters if you're picking a community to join or run

For anyone evaluating Skool as a platform, this closes a gap that used to require external analytics or guesswork. Hosts can now see, in-platform, whether their YouTube channel, Instagram, or a specific affiliate is actually converting into paying members, not just clicks. For members comparing communities, it is also a reminder that this data is starting to shape Skool's own discovery and Growth Boost mechanics elsewhere on the platform, so a host's traffic mix increasingly affects how a community shows up beyond their own audience.
