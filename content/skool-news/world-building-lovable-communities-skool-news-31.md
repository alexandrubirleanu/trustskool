# World Building on Skool: How to Turn Your Community Into a Brand Universe (Skool News #31)

Skool News #31 spends its first half on product updates and member wins, then dedicates its back half to a concept the hosts call "world building": deliberately surrounding a community with mascots, spinoff content, events, and merch so it feels like a universe rather than a single group page.

## What "world building" actually means here

The hosts introduce the idea by turning the camera on Skool's own operation as the case study. On screen, a working document lists the pieces they consider part of Skool's "world": the Skool Bus Tour, an ambassadors program, the Skool Games event, this Skool News show itself, a private owners-only community called Skoolers, a Skool Stories series, a Skool HQ office, a cat mascot, and a line of branded merch (hoodies and beanies). As one host puts it, "there's these other things around it," beyond the core product. The pitch to viewers is that a single Skool group can borrow the same pattern at a smaller scale: a mascot, a recurring show or series, a physical or symbolic touchpoint (their example is a literal school bus), and merchandise all give members something to attach to beyond the content itself. The segment also touches a "Goose world tour" bit, tied to limited-time Antarctica-themed tickets and merchandise orders, as a lighter example of the same instinct: manufacturing lore and running bits that make a community feel alive between posts. The hosts note that even Skool Games, one of their bigger live events, "started out as something small" before growing into a recurring fixture, framing world building as compounding rather than something to launch fully formed.

## The product news before the world-building segment

Before getting to that theme, the episode runs through the usual mix of member spotlights and roadmap items. It opens with Hamza, a 28-year-old community owner who has made over $2 million in two years on Skool and used it to pay off his parents' mortgage, followed by a newer "GOAT" post from an owner earning roughly $400k per month. From there the hosts move into shipped and upcoming platform changes: a livestreaming update increasing video and screenshare size (cutting down on black space) with custom background support already available, and pinning multiple participants plus improved reactions listed as coming soon. The episode also previews three roadmap items with live demos: Subscription Tiers and Webinars, both targeted for an October ETA, and Analytics, targeted for November. A "Skool Bus Project" segment rounds out the update portion before the world-building discussion begins.

## Why this matters if you're running or evaluating a community

For someone choosing between Skool communities, the world-building framing is a useful lens for spotting which groups are being run as a living brand versus a static content dump. A community with its own running jokes, a named mascot, a recurring format, or merch members actually want is doing the same thing Skool does at company scale: it gives people a reason to stick around that isn't purely transactional. For owners, the practical takeaway is that this doesn't require a six-figure production budget. Skool's own examples, an eBay school bus, a cat mascot, a seasonal "world tour" bit, are lightweight and mostly built from consistency and repetition rather than spend. Combined with the roadmap items in this episode (Tiers, Webinars, and Analytics all due within weeks of this recording), it's a reminder that the platform's product direction and its cultural advice are pointed the same way: give members more reasons, and more tools, to stay engaged long after signup.
