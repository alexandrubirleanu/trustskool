# 11 Skool Communities Making $1,000,000+ a Year (And What They Actually Sell)

Episode 58 of Skool News opens with a Discovery algorithm update and an about-page tutorial, then spends its back half doing something rarer: naming actual seven-figure Skool communities by name, niche, and price, drawn from what the hosts say is a pool of "hundreds" of groups on the platform earning over $1,000,000 a year.

## The 11 communities named

The hosts call it "School of the Week: millionaire edition," a random sample pulled from Skool's internal data rather than a ranked top-11. Here's the list, with the pricing and niche details given on air:

- **AI Video Bootcamp** - a $9/month product with no pre-existing audience that went from $0 to $180,000/month in four months.
- **Wealthy Plumber** - "badass business training for plumbers," $197/month.
- **Bulletproof Cycling** - cycling coaching, $99/month, free trial enabled.
- **TFW Global** (Trading For Women) - forex education, $35/month.
- **Eternal Life Tribe** - spirituality with a health component, $55/month.
- **Faceless YouTube HQ** - teaches faceless channel-building; members have reportedly generated 184 million views and matching ad revenue.
- **Broadcast Room** - personal branding, run by Jamie C.
- **Qubecraft** - DevOps career training, positioned as "land a job in tech in 2 to 20 weeks."
- **The Lady Change** (Petra Ginko) - fitness for women, cited elsewhere in the episode at a 17% about-page conversion rate.
- **Confidence and Self-Mastery** - self-improvement, run by Max Person.
- **Six-Figure Barber Mentorship** - teaches barbers to earn $100K/year cutting hair; the founder himself clears over $1M/year.

## The pattern: low ticket, recurring, any niche

The hosts' own takeaway is the interesting part. Several of these run $35-$99/month, not high-ticket coaching, which the hosts use to push back on the assumption that "recurring doesn't work for info products." Pricing spans from $9/month (AI Video Bootcamp) to $197/month (Wealthy Plumber), and niches range from forex trading and DevOps careers to cycling, barbering, and spirituality. The hosts note that money, tech, and AI communities tend to over-index on revenue, but the list is deliberately padded with non-obvious categories, plumbing, hairdressing, spirituality, to make the point that a seven-figure Skool doesn't require a hyped niche, just a specific, well-packaged offer.

## The two mechanics behind the growth: free trials and the about page

The rest of the episode is really about why these communities convert, and it ties back to two concrete Skool features. First, free trials: the hosts state that 7-day free trials average a 40-50% conversion rate to paid, and that in "the vast majority" of cases tested, groups with a free trial outperform those without one. The one exception flagged is when a group is already so cheap or in-demand that friction barely matters, AI Video Bootcamp's $9/month price point is cited as a case where the hosts skip the trial entirely. Second, the about page and group card, the two things a prospective member sees before joining, are described as the single highest-leverage thing to optimize as Discovery sends more traffic to the platform (the hosts cite roughly a million visits a day to Discovery). Their concrete test: show your about page to someone with zero context and see if they instantly understand what the group is about from the image and headline alone, before reading any body text.

## Why this matters if you're evaluating a Skool community

For anyone browsing Skool.com deciding which paid community to join, or benchmarking one you already run, this episode is a useful reality check on what "successful" actually looks like on the platform: modest monthly prices, a working free trial, and a tightly packaged about page, more often than a splashy niche or a huge price tag. It's also a reminder that self-reported revenue screenshots and on-air shoutouts aren't the same as verified growth data, which is the gap a platform-wide leaderboard is built to close.
