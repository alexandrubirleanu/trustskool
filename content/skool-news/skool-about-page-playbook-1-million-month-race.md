# Inside Skool's Race to $1M/Month: The About-Page Playbook Behind Its Biggest Communities

Episode 56 of Skool News opens with hosts Andrew Kirby and Skool CEO Sam Ovens tracking a real race playing out on the platform: two of Skool's top community owners, Spencer and Logan, are neck and neck to become the first to hit $1,000,000 a month. That framing sets up the real subject of the episode, which spends most of its runtime on what Skool calls "packaging," the about page and group card that decide whether a visitor actually joins.

## Why packaging gets its own episode

The hosts' argument is blunt: traffic gets people to your Skool page, but packaging is what converts them once they arrive. A weak about page or a generic group card bleeds visitors that a paid community worked hard to earn, whether that traffic came from YouTube, Instagram, or Skool's own Discovery feed. Rather than talk in the abstract, the episode pulls up five real about pages on screen and walks through what each one does right, from headline clarity to how the preview video is framed to what proof (member counts, rankings, testimonials) sits above the fold.

One example shown in detail: Daniel Riley's AI Video Bootcamp, a $9/month community with roughly 20,200 members. Its about page leads with a short demo video, then stacks concrete credibility markers directly underneath, labeling itself "Top 1% Community on Skool" and "Ranked #1 AI Video/Image Course on Skool." The hosts hold it up as a template for how much of the "why join" case should be made before a visitor even scrolls.

## Two real communities, two different monetization models

The episode backs the about-page advice with real case studies pulled from Skool's own Discovery leaderboard, not hypotheticals.

The first is Samuel B. Lee, M.D.'s Eternal Life Tribe, a spirituality-niche community charging $55/month across roughly 4,100 members. On the Discovery screen the hosts pull up mid-episode, Eternal Life Tribe shows real monthly recurring revenue north of $155,000, putting it well ahead of category neighbors like Reality Revolution and Harmony, both sitting in the high $20,000s. The hosts' read is simple: the about page reads like a single, focused pitch, not a bundle of unrelated promises.

The second is Michael Bar's Studicata, a law-school and bar-exam prep community with a very different structure: free to join, with roughly 18,600 members, monetized entirely through inside upsells, video and outline access at $29/month, personalized essay grading at $99/month, and a one-time $995 "Back to Basics" bar review course. The hosts note Studicata is Skool's all-time #1 ranked "self-improvement" community and cite Michael Bar being recognized by Inc. as the 81st fastest-growing education company in the US, a case for free-entry-plus-upsell working just as well as a straight paid gate when the packaging clearly earns trust before asking for anything.

## What this means if you're evaluating a Skool community

For anyone deciding whether to join, or already running, a Skool community, the throughline is that the about page isn't cosmetic, it's the actual sales page, and Skool's own Discovery rankings increasingly reward communities that treat it that way. The leaderboard screen shown in this episode, real MRR numbers ranked side by side within a category, is also a reminder of how opaque that data normally is to anyone outside a community's own admin dashboard. Member counts and glossy about pages tell you what a community wants you to see; they don't tell you whether it's actually growing, plateauing, or quietly losing paying members.
