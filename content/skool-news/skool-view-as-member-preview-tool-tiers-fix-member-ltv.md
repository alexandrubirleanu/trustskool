# Skool's New View-as-Member Preview Tool, a Tiers Fix, and Member LTV (Skool News #35)

Episode #35 of Skool News, released on 11/11, walks through three shipped features (a universal view-as-member preview tool, member lifetime value tracking, and a cleanup of Tiers) before pivoting to community stories and a segment on what actually drives engagement inside a Skool community.

## See exactly what your members see

The headline feature is a "view as member" preview mode, accessible from a course, the About page, the members list, the Plans page, or a calendar event via the three-dot menu or a small eye icon. Click it, and an admin can toggle between Standard, Premium, and VIP to see precisely what content unlocks, what a paywall prompt looks like, and what members can't see, like billing status or cancellation controls that admins normally view by default. Before this, confirming that gating worked correctly meant creating a second account or opening an incognito tab. It's desktop-only for now, which the hosts note is deliberate since most admins configure tiers and courses from a desktop anyway.

## Member LTV lands, aggregate analytics coming next

Skool now surfaces lifetime value (LTV), the total a member has paid across subscriptions and one-time purchases, directly on their profile and in the members CSV export alongside price and tier. Right now it's per-member only; the hosts flagged that community-wide LTV trends over time are coming with a broader analytics update, without giving a date.

## Tiers get a real fix, not just a patch

This episode resolves confusion that Skool itself created with Tiers. There is no longer a "free tier." There are only three tiers, Standard, Premium, and VIP, and "free" is simply a price you can set on any tier (usually Standard). Every member now defaults to Standard automatically, rather than sitting in an undefined non-tier state as before, and admins can manually reassign any member's tier from the Membership panel, useful for comping a partner or fixing a billing edge case. Premium and VIP members now show a small tier badge next to their name in the members list, so owners can spot upgrades at a glance. An admin notification email is also rolling out for tier or plan upgrades. The hosts say this shipped the same day as the episode and should reach most accounts within a day or two of the 2025-11-11 publish date.

## Community signals worth noting

Tony Robbins and Dean Graziosi's "AI Advantage" community was called out as a fast-growing school with roughly 59,000 members, a notable data point for anyone tracking which large-name creators are moving onto the platform. On the lighter side, two members who met at a Skool Games event got engaged, and members contributed fan art, including a Simpsons-style illustration from a former Simpsons animator now running an art-instruction community, and custom mugs from a pottery-community owner.

## The real lesson: value doesn't equal engagement

The most useful segment for anyone running a community isn't a feature at all. The hosts discuss a member post arguing that pure "value" posts often get ignored while a casual selfie or fun update pulls in far more engagement. Their take: too much value-only content can make a community feel like homework, and genuine engagement comes from members talking to each other, with value as a byproduct rather than the goal. Skool's own internal community reportedly created a dedicated "fun" category specifically to signal that lighter, non-value posts are welcome.

## Why this matters if you're evaluating Skool communities

None of this is a platform overhaul, but it's a meaningful maturity pass on Tiers, which is now Skool's primary monetization mechanic. If you're running a paid community, the preview tool and tier badges remove real guesswork about what members actually experience. If you're evaluating which communities are worth joining, the engagement point is a useful filter: a school that leaves room for members to just talk to each other, not just consume drip-fed value, tends to be the one that's actually alive.
