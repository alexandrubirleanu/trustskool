# Skool Games Q4 2025: What Hormozi and Skool's CEO Told the Winners About Growth, AI, and Traffic

This is the full, largely unedited recording of the Q4 2025 Skool Games winners event: a roughly two-hour fireside chat where Alex Hormozi and Skool co-founder/CEO Sam Ovens sit with a room of top-earning community owners, several of whom share their own channels and numbers from the floor.

## Talent beats polish now that AI is free

Hormozi opens by relaying a conversation with a community owner whose members used to differentiate themselves with polished ebooks and graphics. Since AI made that kind of production quality free and instant, that edge is gone. His point: when execution is commoditized, the thing that's left to compete on is talent, actual skill at the thing your community teaches, plus personality. He ties this to an 80/20 view of business: becoming exceptional at your core craft does more for you than almost any marketing tactic, because it's the one thing AI can't hand a competitor overnight.

## Skool's business model, in Hormozi's words

Asked what actually makes money for most people in the room, Hormozi reduces it to three steps: promote, point people to your about page, and deliver once they pay. No funnel software, no complex sales motion for anyone under roughly seven figures. It's a deliberately blunt answer aimed at people who overbuild their acquisition process instead of just repeating the same simple loop more often, across more channels.

## Where signups actually come from, straight from the winners

The most useful stretch for anyone evaluating Skool is the traffic discussion. Several panelists say plainly that Skool's own Discovery and group search account for a small share of their signups. One creator running a TikTok Live-based community says people join mid-stream without ever reading the about page, because they already trust the host as the expert; the about page is a formality for traffic that's already warm. Another panelist, in the real-estate wholesaling niche, says Instagram plus ManyChat automation, sending a post and DMing the join link, is their highest-converting channel. When Hormozi polls the room on who else uses Instagram plus ManyChat this way, nearly every hand goes up; one respondent adds that swapping the plain link for a short PDF of actionable resources with the offer link embedded converts noticeably better, a tweak they've now run across 500 of their own posts.

A separate point worth flagging for anyone building a premium community: one panelist notes that members brought in from an email list or social channels convert and retain worse than members who were already engaged on Skool first, because they haven't built trust in the platform itself yet. For a paid group's profitability, the panelist argues warmth toward Skool as a destination matters almost as much as warmth toward the creator.

## Skool is testing in-person events too

Sam Ovens shares that Skool itself has been running small, ticketed IRL dinners for creators this month in London, Dubai, New York, and LA, roughly 30 people per event at about $150 a ticket, with plans to scale toward 250-person gatherings. His stated reasoning: as AI lets people do more from home, isolation is becoming a real problem, and in-person connection is part of what community operators should be building toward, not just an occasional bonus.

## Why Sam Ovens says Skool beats Circle and Kajabi

Directly asked how Skool became a better product than Circle or Kajabi, Ovens says there's no hidden mechanism: he optimized for product quality over revenue, growth, or valuation from the start, benchmarking Skool against products he personally holds to a high bar, naming Google, Chrome, the iPhone, and YouTube as his reference points. It's a simple answer, but it's a direct, on-record explanation from Skool's own CEO of what he believes the platform's edge actually is.

For anyone running or evaluating a Skool community, the throughline across the whole event is that Skool's internal Discovery is a secondary channel at best for most creators; external platforms plus a simple, repeatable offer structure still do the heavy lifting.
