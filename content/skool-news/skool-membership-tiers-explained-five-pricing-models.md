# Skool Membership Tiers Explained: 5 New Pricing Models (Skool News #33)

Skool News #33 announces Membership Tiers, a rebuild of how Skool communities can charge for access. Instead of one flat price, a group can now run free, subscription, freemium, multi-tier, or one-time pricing, and lock specific courses, calendar events, and other benefits behind whichever tier a member is on.

## Five business models, not one price

The episode walks through Skool's new group-settings screen, where owners pick a pricing model from five options: **Free**, **Subscription** (the old monthly/annual charge-everyone model), **Freemium** (free to join, with one or two paid upgrade tiers available inside), **Tiers** (two or three paid tiers shown immediately at signup), and **One-time** (a single payment instead of a recurring charge, aimed at lifetime-access courses, pop-up groups, and seminars).

The demo group used to illustrate this, a joke "$1 Club," sets up Standard, Premium, and VIP tiers at $1, $2, and $3 a month (or $10/$20/$30 billed annually), with each tier adding more: community access at the bottom, then training and a novelty t-shirt, up to a top tier throwing in a weekly hangout call and a "Hormozi's number" gag benefit. Playful example aside, it shows the real mechanic: each tier is a distinct price with its own list of benefits, and owners can add or remove benefits per tier at any time.

## Freemium and Tiers behave differently at signup

This is the detail creators evaluating the update most need: **Freemium** and **Tiers** are not the same flow. With Freemium, the signup process looks identical to a plain free group, no pricing is shown up front, and members discover paid upgrades later from inside courses, the calendar, settings, or a `/plans` page; free trials aren't available on this path. With Tiers, the pricing options are shown immediately during signup, a prospective member picks a tier (or upgrades later from the same in-app locations), and free trials can be enabled. Choosing between the two is a real strategic decision about whether you want frictionless free signups or upfront tier selection.

## Locking content by tier, and migrating existing members

Courses and calendar events can now be restricted per tier, the same way they've long supported level-gates and time-delays, so a tier lock can stack with those existing unlock rules rather than replace them. Owners can also gate other benefits (coaching, software, events, merch) by tier, and the members list gets filters to see who's sitting on which tier at a glance.

The rollout also had to solve a real problem: what happens to members who joined before a group switched pricing models? The settings screen shown in the video handles this directly, "Previous paid members" and "Previous free members" each get mapped to a selected tier during migration, so an owner isn't stuck manually reassigning an existing member base or accidentally locking out people who already paid.

## Trials, billing, and affiliate payouts

Free trials on tiered plans can be turned on per tier and are gated by card verification, capped at one trial per user to limit abuse. Billing periods are configurable per tier too (monthly only, annual only, or both), and Skool prorates automatically when a member upgrades mid-cycle or switches to annual, so nobody loses paid-for time by moving up a tier. Affiliate commissions carry over across this system as well: a referral is locked in at signup, so if someone joins on a free tier through an affiliate link and upgrades to paid months later, the original referrer still gets credit, under a 14-day last-touch attribution window.

## Why this matters if you're evaluating a Skool community

Tiers turn pricing into a signal. A community running Standard/Premium/VIP with genuinely different content at each level is telling you something about how seriously it treats packaging and retention, versus a single flat paywall. If you're a member deciding whether to join or upgrade, check what's actually gated behind each tier before you pay, since Skool leaves that entirely up to the individual owner to configure well or poorly.
