# Skool's New Spam-Risk Auto-Approval Fix and Multi-Person Live Call Pinning, Explained

Skool News #42 covers two shipped features (a smarter spam-risk filter for auto-approved groups and multi-person pinning on live calls), a roadmap update centered on Discovery, and two Skool of the Week spotlights showing how small niche communities actually get their members.

## Auto-approval no longer lets high-risk members straight in

Large free groups often turn on Skool's "Instant Approval" setting so hundreds of daily membership requests don't pile up. The tradeoff has always been that instant approval accepts everyone indiscriminately, spam accounts included. Skool has now layered its existing spam risk-score system on top of auto-approval: even with instant approval turned on, members flagged with a high risk score won't be auto-approved. Instead, they're held back in the membership requests queue for a human to review manually, while everyone else still gets waved through instantly. If you run a large free community and start noticing membership requests again despite having auto-approval on, this is why, and the hosts note nearly all of those held-back requests should show a high spam-risk flag. The team says they'll keep expanding the factors that feed into this risk score and apply it across more parts of the platform over time.

## Pin multiple people at once on live calls

Skool live rooms previously only let a host pin one person to the main view. Now hosts can pin two or more people simultaneously, in both the live room and the resulting recording, which is useful for interview-style calls, panel discussions, or spotlighting a specific member while the rest of the group stays visible. It works exactly the way pinning already worked, just no longer capped at a single participant, and the demo in the episode shows how fast Skool's live-to-recording pipeline has gotten: go live, record, pin and unpin people, end the call, and the finished recording post is ready to publish within seconds.

## What's next: Discovery is the headline priority for 2026

The roadmap segment lists several items in progress: continued expansion of the risk-score system, notification filtering by type (so you can view only membership requests, only reported posts, etc., instead of one merged feed), and a moderation tools push aimed at reducing spam and easing the burden on admins. The most anticipated item is Traffic Sources, a feature showing exactly where members come from, which the hosts say has been in development for six months and is expected to ship around the start of February. Beyond that, the team says Discovery, meaning search results, category structure, and the ranking algorithm, is the main focus for the year, aimed at helping more communities surface and get found. Multi-language and multi-currency support for Skool's international user base is also on the list.

## Skool of the Week: two very different niches, two different growth engines

The episode's two spotlighted communities are a useful reminder that "cool community" doesn't mean big. Paula Carnell's free community, Spiritual Beekeeping, combines beekeeping with spiritual wellness content, runs a freemium tier structure, and has 186 members, with traffic-source data showing 45% of signups attributed to the Skool Network itself rather than outside promotion. Jack Wood's Shredders University, a free-plus-paid community teaching electric guitar shredding, generates roughly $4,000/month, driven mostly by Instagram (67% of traffic), with Facebook (12%) and Skool Network (7%) filling in the rest. Both follow the same playbook the hosts keep repeating: post content in a niche you're genuinely passionate about, put the Skool link in your bio, start free, and convert to paid once you understand what members actually want.

The rest of the episode is culture, not features: an in-person Skool Festival happening in Germany on February 28th, a story of two members who met and got engaged inside a DJ-focused community, member testimonials about "coffee hour" calls (no agenda, just a standing weekly hangout) working better than expected, and Skool's community map helping local members find each other for meetups.
