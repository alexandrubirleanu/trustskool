In the first Skool News episode of 2026, the hosts explain why this year's platform theme is "Discovery," then walk through real traffic-source breakdowns from four live communities showing how much of their member growth already comes from Skool's own network rather than the creator's own audience.

## Why "Discovery," and why now

Skool tends to pick a yearly theme. 2024 was the Alex Hormozi partnership plus the first Skool Games, which the hosts credit with a period of explosive growth. 2025 was "10x better, 10x cheaper": native video, native live streaming, built-in analytics, membership tiers, and the $9/month starter plan, which together cut the effective cost of running a community roughly 11x.

For 2026, the stated theme is Discovery. The logic the hosts give: once a platform handles everything you need to run an online community business, and the price is already low, the next bottleneck is members. So this year Skool says it's investing in better search results and suggestions, routing more traffic into the Discover tab and into individual groups, and giving community owners clearer analytics on exactly where their members come from, whether that's YouTube, Instagram, Facebook ads, or the Skool network itself.

## The numbers behind the pitch

To back this up, the episode pulls real traffic-source data straight from four communities' built-in analytics:

- **Nate Herk (AI automation)** runs a 226,000-member free community and a $94/month paid community with 3,100 members, for roughly $238,000/month. In the trailing 30 days, his free community picked up 7,000 members from YouTube versus 15,000 from Skool's own network, meaning Skool accounted for 63% of his new members.
- **Kim Thompson (Circular Machine Knitting)** has a 1,100-member free community. Over 30 days, Facebook brought in 164 members and Skool's network brought in 138, about 37% of her total.
- **David Mincy (Straight from the Coil, metal detecting)** has a 49-member free community fed by a 4,000-subscriber YouTube channel. Skool's network was responsible for 36% of his members in the last 30 days.
- **Gillian Berry (Club Raw, raw vegan recipes)** launched a paid community, $30/month, only weeks before this episode and is already making about $5,200/month. Her traffic is still dominated by YouTube and Instagram, with Skool's network at only around 9% so far.

## The "tailwind" framing

The hosts describe this pattern as a tailwind rather than an engine: you still have to get your community moving yourself, through a link in bio, posts on channels you already have, or just inviting people from your contacts. But once there's some momentum, Skool's own network appears to start pushing additional, unprompted members into the group on top of whatever you're driving externally. They cite roughly 30% of new members platform-wide currently coming from Skool's own network rather than external traffic, and say that share is increasing. They also mention niche communities picking up members purely through on-platform search before any outside promotion at all, using a ukulele-focused group as their example, while cautioning that this is inconsistent right now and far from fully built out.

## Two other things worth knowing

Q1 2026's Skool Games, the quarterly leaderboard contest that rewards the fastest-growing paid communities by category, with top-five finishers flown to Skool's LA office, started the day this episode was recorded. Nate Herk gets a shoutout for reportedly winning all four quarters of the 2025 Skool Games. And for anyone newer to the platform, the recommended starting point is the Skool 101 course in the classroom, plus using Discover to join a handful of communities in your own areas of interest and browsing other members' profiles to see what groups they belong to before launching your own.

## Why this matters if you're evaluating communities

Member counts and MRR figures look impressive on their own, but the traffic mix behind them, how much is owned audience versus platform-driven discovery, says a lot about how repeatable and durable that growth really is.
