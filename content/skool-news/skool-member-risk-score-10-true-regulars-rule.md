# Skool's Member Risk Score and the '10 True Regulars' Rule for Real Community

Episode #41 of Skool News mixes real platform updates, most notably a newly live member risk score and a preview of an incoming Traffic Sources dashboard, with the channel's most emotional Skool of the Week segment yet: a small photography community whose members made a tribute video for their founder, which the hosts use to unpack exactly what makes a Skool community actually work.

## What's new on the platform

The headline shipped feature is a **member risk score** on membership requests: when you review someone asking to join your group, Skool now flags a spam/risk signal next to their request so you can spot likely bad actors before letting them in. The hosts call this an early "V1," with more data points and a smarter detection model coming; they deliberately avoided detailing which signals feed the score, since spelling it out just teaches spammers how to route around it.

Bigger news is still on the roadmap. A **Traffic Sources** feature is in active development (targeted for early February) after roughly six months of work. It'll live in a new Growth tab in community settings, showing about-page visits, signups, conversion rate, and new monthly recurring revenue over the trailing 30 days, broken down by source: affiliates, the Skool Network, Instagram, YouTube, Facebook ads, and more. Attribution will also work at the individual member level, so you can see exactly where each person in your members list came from, not just an aggregate percentage.

Moderation is next in line after that. Planned upgrades include a smarter auto-approve system that uses the risk score to auto-approve low-risk applicants, auto-decline high-risk ones, and hold ambiguous cases for manual review, plus an optional SMS verification requirement for posting, commenting, or DMing, meant to stop banned members from simply making a new account. The hosts also flagged work on page load speed (Skool's growth is now adding in two weeks what its first four years took) and improvements to Discovery's search, categories, and ranking system.

## Skool of the Week: a real-world traffic breakdown

This episode's featured community is Underwater Squad, a $9/month freediving and breath-holding coaching group run by a creator named Ge, pulling in roughly $3,000/month using tiers. The hosts used it to preview what the Traffic Sources dashboard will actually show: Instagram (30%), Facebook (20%), YouTube (19%), and the Skool Network (9%), driven by a link stacked in his bio, cover image, and posts across all three platforms.

## The story behind "we cried"

The video's real centerpiece is a post from a member named Glenn, who runs a 72-person paid photography community. His members, unprompted, wrote and recorded an original song and video thanking him for what the community meant to them, and the hosts play it on air. The takeaway isn't the video itself but what it proves: a community doesn't need scale to be meaningful. Seventy people who show up consistently can build something tighter than a group ten times its size.

## The "10 true regulars" framework

That leads into the episode's real thesis: you don't need thousands of members to have a thriving community, you need roughly ten true regulars who show up and engage consistently. The hosts compare it to the "90% lurker" rule seen across the internet: even in a 10,000-person group, ten genuinely active people is often enough to make the whole community feel alive.

Their formula for getting there has four parts: build around a specific shared passion or goal, show up daily as the owner (reading posts, replying, and adjusting your roadmap based on real community feedback), run a low-prep weekly call like a "coffee hour" and post the recording afterward (the hosts note recordings often draw ten times the audience of the live call), and deliberately deepen relationships with the people who keep showing up, escalating from comments to DMs to calls to in-person meetups. Asked directly what his secret was, Glenn's answer was simple: "treat members as people and not as income."
