# Skool Webinars: One-to-Many Live Streaming for Up to 10,000 People, Explained

Skool News #32 announces Skool Webinars, a native one-to-many live-streaming format that sits alongside the existing Skool Call feature, plus a redesign of live-call reactions and an update on the platform's user count, now past 18 million.

## Webinars vs. Skool Calls: a different format, not just a bigger one

Skool already had live video calls, but those are built for collaborative group conversation, everyone on camera, everyone able to jump in. Webinars are structured differently: it's a one-to-many presentation format, where a host broadcasts to an audience rather than running an open discussion. That distinction matters for how you'd actually use it. A weekly coaching call or mastermind session still fits Skool Call. A product launch, a large training session, or a Q&A meant for hundreds or thousands of viewers at once is what Webinars are built for.

Per Skool's own announcement around the feature, the format supports one-to-many live streams with up to 10,000 participants in a single session, positioning it for use cases like live tutorials, big Q&As, product launches, and large-scale community events, the kind of thing creators previously needed a separate tool like Zoom Webinar or StreamYard to run alongside their Skool community.

## How to set one up

Setting up a webinar works the same way as scheduling anything else in Skool: open the Calendar tab, create a new event, and choose "Skool Webinar" as the event type instead of a regular call. When the scheduled time arrives, the host clicks "Join Webinar" to start broadcasting to registered attendees. It's worth noting for anyone budgeting features: according to Skool's help documentation, Webinar is a Pro plan feature, so free-tier or Starter communities won't have this option available without upgrading.

## Reactions get a refresh too

The second half of the episode covers an update to live reactions on both calls and webinars, part of a broader push to make live sessions feel more interactive for large audiences who can't all be unmuted at once. The idea is straightforward: viewers who'd otherwise be passive in a 500- or 5,000-person stream can still signal engagement in real time, which matters for hosts trying to read the room during a big launch or training.

## Context: Hormozi, Logan Paul, and 18 million users

The episode also touches on Skool getting mentioned by Alex Hormozi and Logan Paul, and shares a milestone update: the platform has now crossed 18 million users, with a throwback to when it first hit 1 million. For anyone sizing up Skool as a platform to build on, that trajectory is a useful data point. This isn't a niche community tool anymore; it's a platform that's added double-digit millions of users while continuing to ship features (like large-scale webinars) aimed squarely at creators running paid communities at scale.

## Why this matters if you're evaluating a Skool community

Webinars close a real gap for course creators and coaches who were previously running their community on Skool but hosting launches or big trainings elsewhere. If you're a member (or prospective member) of a Skool community, it's a signal the community's owner can run bigger live events without sending you off-platform. If you run a community yourself, it's one less tool in your stack, assuming you're on the Pro plan. Either way, it's another sign that Skool is building toward being the single hub for content, payments, and now large-scale live events, rather than a plug-in for a stack of other tools.
