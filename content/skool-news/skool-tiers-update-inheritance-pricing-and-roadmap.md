# Skool Tiers Update: Tier Inheritance, CSV Exports, and a $9/Month Plan (Skool News #34)

Episode #34 of Skool News is a deep dive into Skool's membership Tiers feature: a batch of quality-of-life fixes that shipped, several features on the near-term roadmap, an FAQ segment, and real examples of creators using freemium and tiered pricing to monetize a single school.

## What changed: tier inheritance and clearer plan pages

The headline fix addresses a question every multi-tier community owner runs into: do higher tiers automatically get what lower tiers include? The answer, now explicit in the product, is yes. Tiers inherit benefits downward, a member on VIP automatically has everything Premium and Standard members get, without an owner needing to duplicate content or permissions across every tier. Skool also cleaned up the interface language on plan and content pages so it's clearer which membership level unlocks which content, a small change but a meaningful one for members who previously had to guess why something was locked.

Two more shipped updates target admins directly. Creators can now drag-and-drop reorder the benefits listed on a plan page, so the strongest incentive (live coaching calls, a private channel, whatever converts best) can sit at the top instead of buried in a bullet list. And the member CSV export got richer: exports now include price paid per subscriber, tier assignment, and lifetime value, data that used to require stitching together from Stripe manually, now available natively for revenue analysis outside the platform.

## Coming soon: manual assignment, upgrade alerts, and preview mode

Three features were previewed as in-progress. The most requested is the ability to manually assign a member to a tier, useful for comping a partner, honoring a grandfathered price, or fixing a billing edge case without asking the member to re-subscribe. Right behind it is an upgrade notification that fires when a member moves up a tier, so owners know to follow up rather than finding out weeks later. Third is a preview mode: viewing your own community through the eyes of a specific tier, so an owner can confirm a Standard member genuinely can't see Premium-only content before a member reports it as a bug.

## Pricing and FAQ

Skool now offers a $9/month starter plan, lowering the bar for creators who want to launch a school without committing to the $99/month Pro tier while validating an idea. On the FAQ side, the hosts addressed two recurring questions: whether tier names can be customized (relevant if "Standard/Premium/VIP" doesn't match your brand), and whether specific content categories can be gated to a single tier rather than gating entire courses or channels. Neither answer changes what's possible today, but both signal where Skool is thinking about flexibility next.

## Real examples: freemium and stacked pricing

The episode closes with how creators are actually using this. The common freemium pattern: a free tier gets the community feed plus one lead-magnet piece of content, while the paid tier adds full courses, live calls, and bonus material. On the tiered side, some owners run a low-ticket monthly tier alongside a high-ticket mastermind inside the same school, the video cites a $37/month tier paired with a $997/month tier as one real configuration. Both patterns let a single community capture members at very different budgets without running two separate schools.

## Why this matters if you're evaluating Skool communities

None of this is a platform overhaul, it's Skool making an existing monetization tool less clunky and more transparent. If you're a member wondering why you can or can't see something, tier inheritance and clearer plan-page language should reduce that confusion. If you're evaluating which Skool communities are worth joining, freemium-with-a-paid-upsell and stacked low/high-ticket pricing are both signs a community has a deliberate monetization structure rather than a single flat price.
