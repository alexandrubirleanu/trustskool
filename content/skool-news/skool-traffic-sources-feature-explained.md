# Skool's Traffic Sources Feature Explained: How to Actually Get More Members

Episode 46 of Skool News covers a handful of shipped features, an AutoMod update, and a Skool of the Week, but the real substance is a walkthrough of the newly launched Traffic Sources analytics and a channel-by-channel breakdown of how top communities actually get members from YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, and Skool's own Discovery network.

## What else shipped this episode

Two smaller features landed alongside Traffic Sources. First, an annual billing discount: switching a Hobby ($9/month) or Pro ($99/month) plan to annual now gets two months free, with a prorated credit for unused time on the current plan, and you're not locked into annual if you later want to move back to monthly. Second, "Hi-Fi audio" for live calls and webinars, a toggle in call settings that disables noise cancellation and boosts audio quality, aimed specifically at musicians streaming instruments. AutoMod also expanded: the spam risk-score system that already screened membership requests now flags risky people in DMs and in reported posts/comments too. The hosts stress AutoMod evaluates the person's behavior patterns, not the content itself, which is why an apparently harmless comment can still get flagged, and why a "mark as safe" override is planned.

## How Traffic Sources actually works

The headline feature shows where signups come from: Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, Google, direct, affiliates, and the Skool Network itself. The hosts' central point is that this data gets misread constantly. It's tempting to conclude "Instagram is the channel" or "YouTube is the channel," but the data shows most creators have one dominant channel and it's different for everyone, some get 80% of members from Instagram, others 80% from YouTube. The advice: do more of what's already working for you specifically, and don't kill an underperforming channel just because someone else's data says it doesn't work, since blended traffic often helps in ways attribution alone won't show.

The most important idea in the episode is what the hosts call a "tailwind": Skool Network traffic isn't really a separate acquisition channel, it's a reward for driving traffic from elsewhere. The example given is a creator named Evelyn, who gets 44% of signups credited to Skool Network, but only 12% of her actual visitors arrive that way, most of her traffic is Facebook and Instagram. Skool's algorithm promotes communities that already show growth momentum, so external traffic indirectly fuels Skool Network signups. Cut the outside promotion, and Skool Network traffic dries up too. The related question the hosts push community owners to ask isn't "what converts last" but "where did people first find me," since a channel like email or direct traffic that looks self-sustaining is often just downstream of YouTube or Instagram content someone saw months earlier.

## Channel-by-channel growth tactics

For **YouTube**: link in bio, link at the top of every video description (mobile only shows the first line), a pinned comment, and mentioning the community natively on camera, showing calls, wins, and the classroom rather than just a verbal callout. Bulk-editing old video descriptions to add the link is flagged as an easy way to recapture backlog traffic.

For **Instagram**, described as the single biggest traffic source across all of Skool: link in bio plus a ManyChat comment-to-DM automation ("comment the word X and I'll send you the link") is credited as the tactic nearly every top Instagram-driven community is using, since it boosts comment counts (helping distribution) and opens a DM conversation that handles objections.

For **TikTok**: link in bio and, more importantly, going live consistently. One example cited is a Spanish-language tutoring community reportedly earning around $60,000/month largely from daily TikTok livestreams.

For **Skool Discovery**: ranking rewards genuinely active, well-run communities (daily presence, low spam, regular calls) combined with outside promotion driving growth momentum. Being a good-faith member of other communities can help too, but the hosts warn explicitly against gaming Discovery for referral credit, calling it a short-term play that gets penalized once identified.
