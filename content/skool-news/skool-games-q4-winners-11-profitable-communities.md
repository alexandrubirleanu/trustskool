# Skool Games Q4 Winners: 11 Profitable Communities and What's Actually Driving Their Growth

Episode 44 of Skool News recaps the Skool Games Q4 winners event, an in-person meetup where Skool flew out top-performing community owners for a day at company HQ, and uses it to showcase eleven profitable Skool communities across wildly different niches, alongside an early look at the Traffic Sources analytics feature that would later ship platform-wide.

## The eleven communities, and what they're making

Rather than one polished case study, this episode is a rapid tour through the range of niches actually making money on Skool. The revenue figures below were shared on camera as monthly numbers:

- A tennis-coaching community run by a player ranked around 20th in France: $7,000/month
- An AI-focused community: $121,000/month, the largest figure mentioned
- A "connect and collaborate" community: $2,700/month
- A community run by an operator partnered with an NBA player: $7,850 MRR
- PM Mastermind, teaching project management: $59,000/month
- Dave's Guitar World, guitar-solo instruction: $5,600/month
- A financial-freedom and home-buying community: $14,000/month
- A pickleball-coaching community: $41,000/month
- A basketball-coaching community: $47,000/month
- Artist Academy: just under $10,000/month
- Crusade Games, a fitness and habit-building community: $13,000/month

The spread is the point: instruction-based niches (tennis, guitar, pickleball, basketball) sit alongside finance, fitness, and AI communities, and revenue ranges from under $3K to over $120K a month. There's no single "right" niche for Skool, just consistent execution inside whichever one a creator already knows.

## Skool of the Week: three growth patterns worth noting

The episode features three separate "Skool of the Week" spotlights, and each one demonstrates a different acquisition channel:

**Diego Riverier's salsa community** charges $39/month and generates $36,000/month. His traffic breakdown, shown using the platform's then-unreleased Traffic Sources tool, was roughly 50% Instagram, 10% Google search, and 7% Skool Network, with Instagram content driving people to his community's about page.

**John Reed's Astronomy Club** is a free community built around a bestselling author with a background in astrophysics and space systems engineering (and a recurring "space happy hour" call). His traffic mix was closer to 38% YouTube and 28% Skool Network, showing organic platform discovery starting to outweigh outside traffic as a community matures.

**Robin Lewis's Granny Wisdom Hippie Vibes and Crystal Appreciation Society** communities pull 98.6% of their growth from the Skool Network itself. With only 148 members, this is a small-scale example of what the hosts call becoming "Skool native": a community that gets discovered and grows almost entirely inside the platform rather than through external marketing.

The common thread across all three: Instagram and YouTube are described as the two dominant external channels for growing a Skool community, with Skool's internal Discovery network as a secondary or, in mature cases, primary source.

## A preview of Traffic Sources, before it shipped

The Traffic Sources breakdowns used throughout this episode were flagged as an early, unreleased feature being tested internally at the time of filming. It later shipped in full as its own update (covered separately in Skool News #46), giving every community owner a dashboard view of where their signups actually come from.

## Community culture and the affiliate program

The episode closes with lighter platform-culture notes: members getting novelty "Skooler" license plates, a Germany-based member named Calvin hosting a self-organized Dubai meetup after telling his followers "don't wait, host" your own local meetup using Skool's member map, and a live demonstration of Skool's affiliate program. Referring new paying users earns 40% of their recurring subscription for life, meaning referring just three people to a paid plan can make a creator's own Skool subscription free, or profitable. One member, Andrew Crowise, was shown receiving a $6,400 affiliate payout on camera.

For anyone evaluating Skool or already running a community, the throughline of this episode is that profitable Skools span nearly any niche, and growth almost always traces back to a small number of channels: Instagram, YouTube, or the Skool Network itself.
