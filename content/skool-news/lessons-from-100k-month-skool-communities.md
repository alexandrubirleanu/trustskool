# What Skool's $100,000/Month Communities All Have in Common

Episode 63 of Skool News covers three things: new search filters and a security-verification layer for sensitive account actions, a roadmap update touching payouts, languages, freemium, and free trials, and a breakdown of what Skool calls "going platinum," the pattern shared by every community on the platform earning $100,000 or more a month.

## New features and a security layer

Discovery's search results page now has filters for free, paid, free trial, public, private, and language, making it easier to narrow down communities from the search bar rather than scrolling categories. On the security side, Skool has added a verification step for what it calls "sensitive actions," things like deleting a course, adding an admin, changing a group's owner, changing the email on your account, issuing refunds, or cancelling someone's subscription. Trigger one of these and you'll now need to verify with a code before it goes through, a direct response to the fact that real money moves through these communities.

## What's next: payouts, languages, freemium, and trial locks

On the roadmap: global fast payouts (some countries, India among them, currently can't get paid out at all, others get paid slowly), a fully localized UI for Skool's international user base, and new local payment methods and currencies beyond cards. Two changes aimed specifically at conversion are worth flagging for anyone running a paid community. First, freemium groups will start showing pricing plans at signup instead of burying them once someone's already inside. Second, free trials will support locking specific content, a course or even a single page within one, so trial members get a real taste without full access, with a one-click path to unlock everything by upgrading immediately rather than waiting out the trial.

## Growth Boost: stop linking out from your about page

The hosts flagged a change landing within the week: Skool's Discovery algorithm will start penalizing about pages that contain outbound links (to Google Docs, other groups, scheduling tools, etc.), because those links measurably hurt both conversion rate and analytics tracking. Communities without stray links tend to convert better, so this is a case where cleaning up your about page now avoids a ranking hit later. The episode also highlights Evan Sean's GTA Creator Academy, which went from zero audience to 260 paid members in one week purely on a strong cover image and about page.

## The actual $100K/month playbook

The core segment profiles three communities clearing six figures a month: Nick Sarv's Maker School ($180/month, AI automation, ~100% of traffic from a single YouTube channel, $250K/month), YM Baby's trading bootcamp ($50/month, built entirely on Instagram, $150K/month), and Oscar Patel's health community ($39/month, split between Instagram and YouTube, $300K/month at age 20 after fourteen months). The pattern across every $100K+/month community the hosts reviewed: no funnels, no email sequences, no link-in-bio tools stacking ten destinations. Every piece of content points to one link, the Skool about page, every time.

The numbers back this up. The average community earning $100,000/month drives roughly 43,000 monthly visits to its about page, with most landing between 10,000 and 100,000 visits, and their average about-page conversion rate is only 2.1%, lower than most operators would assume necessary. Rather than pausing to optimize that number, these operators just keep sending more traffic, essentially betting on volume over funnel polish. The hosts frame this through Alex Hormozi's optimizer-versus-maximizer distinction: optimizers squeeze small conversion gains from limited traffic, maximizers pour their energy into growing traffic itself and let the simple funnel do the rest. Two more traits showed up consistently: about 80% of these operators post daily (Skool's "fire" streak badge), and none of them outsource their personal replies to a VA or an AI agent, on the logic that people are paying to connect with a real person, not a delegated inbox.
