# Skool Analytics Dashboard Explained: MRR, Cohorts, Cash Flow, and What Good Retention Looks Like

Episode 36 of Skool News is a full walkthrough of "Analytics v1," the new dashboard every community admin gets under Settings. The hosts use anonymized real community data to show what a growing, flat, and shrinking group each look like on the same charts, plus a set of benchmarks pulled from looking at data across the whole platform.

## What's actually in the dashboard

The old admin dashboard showed a handful of one-off numbers. The new one turns each of those into a monthly, historical view going back roughly 12 months:

- **Members**: new, existing, and churned counts per month, plus a running total.
- **MRR**: broken into new, existing, upgrade, downgrade, and churn each month, so tier changes now show up properly instead of being lumped into a single churn figure.
- **Cash flow**: a separate view from MRR, surfacing one-time payments, annual subscriptions (collected in full but spread across 12 months for MRR), affiliate payouts, fees, refunds, and disputes. Previously only MRR was visible, so annual-heavy or affiliate-heavy communities couldn't see their real cash position.
- **Conversion rate**: still shown as a single 30-day number up top, but now chartable day-by-day for 30 days or extended to a full year.
- **Free trials**: trial volume and trial-to-paid conversion rate by month.
- **Retention**: replaces the old "churn" metric. Because it nets out upgrades, downgrades, reactivations, and churn, retention can exceed 100% in a given month if expansion revenue outpaces losses.
- **Unit economics**: ARPU (average revenue per user per month) and LTV, useful for figuring out paid-acquisition payback periods.

Free communities don't get MRR or revenue retention, but they do get **engagement cohorts**, which track what percentage of members who joined in a given month are still returning and viewing pages 30, 60, 90+ days later.

## Reading cohorts without getting misled

The most useful part of the episode is the explanation of cohorted versus aggregate retention. A community can show 90% aggregate retention while actually losing 25% of a given month's revenue in month one, 15% in month two, and roughly 6% in month three, then flattening to near-zero loss after that. The aggregate number blends old, stable customers with new ones still in their risky first 90 days, which can make retention look better or worse than it really is depending on how much you're currently growing.

The dashboard color-codes each cohort against the platform-wide average (green above, red below), and the hosts note the real signal isn't short-term retention, it's whether a cohort's monthly loss curve flattens. Communities that keep compounding tend to look average or slightly below average in month one, then pull sharply ahead by month ten or twelve. One example hit roughly 50% revenue retention at the one-year mark on 100% monthly billing, no annual plans at all.

## The benchmarks worth remembering

A few reference points came up repeatedly: churn is almost always steepest in month one, then month two, then largely flattens around the 90-day mark, meaning a member who sticks past three months is likely to stick long-term. On engagement, a community where roughly 40% of members are still actively returning a full year after joining was called genuinely rare and very good, most apps, sites, and YouTube channels lose the large majority of new users within a year. And with small sample sizes (little traffic, few signups), conversion rate can swing wildly day to day, from 0% to 40%+, so the hosts warn against reacting to short-term dips after an about-page tweak; a month of data is the minimum before drawing conclusions.

## What's shipping next

This is explicitly "v1." The hosts previewed v1.5 (an engagement tab tracking active users, posts, comments, and likes over time, plus in-month projections for MRR and retention) as a possible December 2025 release, and v2 (traffic source attribution, showing what share of members come from Instagram, YouTube, Google, ads, or the Skool Network) targeted for early 2026, with no firm date committed on either.
