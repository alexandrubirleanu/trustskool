# Skool's New Discovery Algorithm Explained: Trending Replaces Top as the Default Sort

In episode #57 of Skool News, the hosts walk through the biggest change to Skool's Discovery page in a while: a new default sort based on 24-hour trending momentum instead of raw size, plus a new filter menu, a roadmap tease that includes an unnamed "secret project," and a gossip segment about owners who say Skool itself is sending them paying customers.

## Discovery's default sort just changed

Until this update, Discovery always looked roughly the same: it defaulted to "top," meaning the biggest or most active groups platform-wide, and those groups tended to stay parked there indefinitely. As the hosts put it: "it always kind of looked the same... it was boring, so you tended not to come back and check it."

The new default is "trending," calculated from rolling 24-hour momentum rather than absolute size. Because that number recalculates constantly, the groups surfacing on Discovery change daily, and the hosts say it changed their own behavior: they now check Discovery every day, something neither did before. The goal is for Discovery to answer "what's hot on Skool right now," not "what's always been big."

One effect worth noting for smaller communities: the trending sort surfaced a "mobility and injury prevention" group the hosts said they'd never heard of, in "a tiny little niche," ranking it among Skool's top groups purely on momentum. Categories are trending-sorted too, and the hosts flagged AI, work-from-home, gut health, and even a cat-drawing community showing up on the trending home page.

## "Top" isn't gone, it's now your progress metric

The old "top" sort still exists, it's just no longer the default. You reach it via Filter → Sort → Top, and it still ranks groups by overall size and activity as a numeric rank (the hosts demoed a group ranked 3,748th). Quietly alongside this, Skool tightened the ranking algorithm to make it "much harder to game." Genuinely active communities should see their rank hold steady or improve; groups relying on shortcuts should see theirs drop. Rank is now framed as a progression metric, tracking whether a community is truly growing, rather than a leaderboard vulnerable to gaming.

## New filter menu, plus a roadmap tease

Discovery also got a new filter menu: public vs. private, free vs. paid vs. free-trial, and language, including a view of what's trending within a given language (Spanish was demoed). Combined with the improved Discovery search shipped just before this and ongoing attribution-accuracy work, Discovery is clearly getting invested in as an acquisition channel, not just a browse page. Looking further out, the team confirmed native language and currency support are both in active planning, and teased an unnamed "secret project" with zero details given beyond the name.

## Gossip: is Skool sending owners free customers?

The episode closes with owners reporting unexplained wins: one described waking up to a new paid member he'd never talked to, who said in a DM he'd "stumbled upon your community while searching for SaaS keywords on Skool," crediting the new Discovery search. Another owner said she saw her own community appear in what looked like a Skool ad despite not running any ads herself, and logged several sales in the following 24 hours she attributed to "Skool network" traffic, with other owners confirming similar experiences in the comments. Neither host had a firm explanation, but the pattern fits Skool actively promoting communities through its own channels.

For anyone evaluating or running a Skool community, the takeaway is that Discovery ranking, and organic reach with it, is now driven by momentum rather than static size, changing how smaller or newer communities can realistically compete for visibility.
