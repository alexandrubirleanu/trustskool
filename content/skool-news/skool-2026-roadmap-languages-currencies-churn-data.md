# Skool's 2026 Roadmap: Languages, Currencies, and the Churn Numbers Sam Ovens Revealed

Skool CEO Sam Ovens and Alex Hormozi sat down together at the Q1 2026 Skool Games awards event, and instead of a keynote, they opened the floor to community owners for almost two hours of live Q&A. Buried in that conversation are some real product signals worth knowing if you run, or are evaluating, a Skool community.

## Discovery and tracking are the current priority

Ovens was direct about where Skool's engineering effort is going right now: distribution. "The main thing really is distribution," he told the room, "we want to start really focusing on discovery." That covers the search results ranking, what the platform suggests on the homepage, and the member-source attribution tracking Skool rolled out to owners, which he said took roughly six months to build and is already being rebuilt again for better accuracy. Ovens admitted the team was genuinely surprised by how well organic discovery was already working even before they had the tracking in place to measure it, which is part of why it's now getting more direct investment.

## What's shipping next: languages, then currencies

On the roadmap question, Ovens confirmed the order of operations: multi-language support is coming before multi-currency and alternative payment methods. He said he was "honestly shocked" at how many non-English-speaking users already run successful communities on a platform that's currently English-only and USD-only, citing Spanish, French, and Chinese as the biggest non-English usage segments. Currencies and payment methods (he name-checked PayPal, Pix, and WeChat Pay as candidates) are next in line, but he called the currency question "complicated as hell" and said he's still working out implementation approaches with owners directly rather than committing to a specific design yet.

## The churn numbers, straight from platform data

The most concrete data point in the session came from Ovens describing platform-wide churn: retention is steep in the first stretch, then flattens hard, dropping to roughly 2% monthly churn across all communities once a member passes the six-month mark. His practical takeaway for owners was to obsess over the first 30 and 90 days of a new member's experience, and specifically to get members past "level 3" in their community, since churn likelihood drops sharply once someone crosses that threshold.

## How Skool decides what to keep or cut

A member asked about adding gamification and badges beyond the existing leaderboards, a feature Ovens didn't commit to but engaged with seriously. More telling was his answer on a different feature question: Skool has quietly removed things before (he cited the old pinned-post limit) without announcing it, specifically to see whether anyone notices or complains before deciding whether to keep it gone. "The only way you can really learn most things is by subtraction," as the conversation put it, capturing Skool's low-drama, test-and-observe approach to shipping and cutting features rather than announcing every change.

## Hormozi's own growth stack, for context

Since Hormozi co-hosted, he also shared how his own high-ticket Skool community sources members: Skool's own discovery and referrals are his top two channels, with only a small amount of paid ad spend layered on top. He mentioned running an AI-driven text-based sales system for that funnel, and was candid that voice AI still isn't reliable enough for this use case after two years of internal testing, largely for infrastructure reasons like pickup rates and call routing rather than the language model itself.
