# Skool's New Leaderboard Levels and Freemium Signup Update, Explained

Skool News #65 covers two real platform changes worth knowing about if you run or are evaluating a Skool community: a new leveling system built into the leaderboards, and a freemium signup flow that now surfaces paid plans immediately. It also digs into what the leaderboard data reveals about how top earners actually drive traffic.

## The leaderboards now have levels and badges

Skool's leaderboards (visible in the Schoolers community) are now built around a leveling system tied to monthly recurring revenue, or MR. Hosts unlock status emojis as they cross thresholds: 3K, 10K, 30K, 100K, 300K, and 1M MR. A brand-new "clover" emoji marks the 3K level, and 100K unlocks a secret club. Physical awards are also coming for the 100K, 300K, and 1M tiers.

Leaderboards can be filtered by category (money, tech, spirituality, health, etc.), showing rank overall and within a niche, plus a new traffic column alongside earnings. You need at least $1 in MR to appear at all.

One clarification worth flagging: none of this is a one-time achievement. Drop below 100K MR and you lose club access; trophies require holding a revenue level for a period, not just spiking once. The hosts were explicit that the point is to nudge builders toward sustainable MR and real traffic, not short-term stunts to win a leaderboard slot.

## Freemium communities now show paid plans at signup

Previously, joining a free-tier Skool community meant clicking in and dealing with pending approval, with any paid upsell surfacing later, if at all. Now, clicking "join" on a freemium community shows the plans page immediately, so people who already want to upgrade can do it on the spot.

This matters beyond conversion rate. Skool had been scoring paid-gated communities as more "efficient" than freemium ones for Growth Boost and ad placement, since asking for payment converts a higher share of buyers. That gap should narrow now that freemium signups convert better, meaning freemium communities should start seeing more discovery traffic and ad placement. A more prominent in-app upgrade prompt for members who chose free is coming next.

Other roadmap items mentioned: locking specific courses or lessons for free-trial users to cut down on freeloading, faster global payouts, additional language support, and continued work on the efficiency score, which Skool says will soon explain exactly why a community's score is low and what to fix.

## What the leaderboard data says about traffic

The hosts pulled one consistent thread from the top earners: nearly all of their external traffic (YouTube, Instagram, TikTok) points back to their Skool community, via bio links, video descriptions, and on-screen mentions. They demoed a free tool, traffic.cendalliance.com.au, that scans a creator's last 50 YouTube videos and flags how many don't link to their Skool, estimating traffic left on the table. One example in the video funnels 49 of 50 videos to Skool and sits fifth on the leaderboard at roughly $224K a month.

A second lesson: how you mention the community matters as much as whether you link it. One host cited a creator who added a 20-second on-camera plug and got five times the signups from the same view count, and another who tripled traffic just by rewriting a vague "check out my Skool" line into a specific benefit statement.

A counter-example on not overbuilding: Gabe Bolt launched his "1% Club" community with a single 90-second phone video, no course, no PDF library, and no free trial. He hit a 5% paid conversion rate and 200 members in the first week. The takeaway: people joined him, not a curriculum, and building in public alongside early members can outperform a polished launch.

The episode closes with three "school of the week" picks showing how varied winning niches can be: Cosmogonia (sacred geometry, $0 to $12K/month off one livestream), a gut-health community at $28K/month, and a $4/month "Crystal Clubhouse" built around in-person crystal-digging trips.
