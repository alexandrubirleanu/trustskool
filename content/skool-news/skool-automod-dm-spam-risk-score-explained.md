# Skool's AutoMod Sneak Peek: How Skool Is Fighting Spam, Scams, and DM Fraud

Episode #43 of Skool News covers two related moves against spam on the platform: a spam risk score that now shows up directly on DMs, and a first look at AutoMod, a system built to catch high-risk accounts before they can spam or scam other members.

## The DM spam risk score

The first piece is already live: a spam risk score attached to direct messages. Instead of relying purely on member reports after the fact, Skool surfaces a risk signal on incoming DMs so recipients get a heads-up before they engage with an unfamiliar sender. For anyone who runs a community with an active chat feature, that's a meaningful shift. DMs are the channel where scam pitches, fake "collab" offers, and impersonation attempts tend to land first, precisely because they happen outside the visibility of posts and comments an admin might otherwise moderate. Putting a risk indicator in front of the member receiving the message, rather than only giving admins after-the-fact reporting tools, pushes the first line of defense to the moment it actually matters.

## The sneak peek: AutoMod

The bigger story in the episode is the preview of AutoMod, described as a system that automatically flags suspicious and AI-generated content for review rather than requiring an admin to catch it manually. At the time of the episode this was framed as a coming feature, not something every community had access to yet.

Skool's own Help Center has since published documentation on AutoMod that fills in what it does once active: it flags high-risk users, and by extension the posts and comments those users publish, and surfaces them for admins to review rather than removing anything automatically. Skool's guidance describes the system as working "by looking at different signals to identify high-risk users" without publishing the exact signals, which is standard practice for anti-abuse systems (publishing the exact detection logic makes it trivial to route around).

What is public is the recommended admin response once someone gets flagged, and it differs depending on membership status:

- **Free members**: ban from the group and block them in chat.
- **Paid members**: process a refund, remove them from the group, and block them in chat.

Admins can also report flagged accounts directly to Skool's support team, which suggests AutoMod is meant to feed a broader trust-and-safety pipeline rather than operate as an isolated, community-level filter.

## Why this matters if you run or evaluate a Skool community

Spam and scam accounts are a tax on every open community platform, and they get more costly, not less, as a community's member count and DM volume grow. A risk score on DMs and an automated flagging layer on posts and comments both shift work away from admins manually policing every new signup, which matters most for larger communities where a single admin can't realistically review every profile by hand.

For anyone evaluating which Skool communities are worth joining or which ones are genuinely well-run, moderation infrastructure is a quieter but real signal. A platform actively investing in automated spam and scam detection is one where a paid membership is less likely to sit next to a wave of fake accounts DMing members with off-platform pitches. It's also a reminder that the health of a community depends on both the admin's own moderation habits and the platform-level tools they have to work with, which is exactly why growth and engagement data alone don't tell the whole story of whether a community is worth your time or money.
