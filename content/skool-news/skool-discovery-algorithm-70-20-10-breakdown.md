# Skool's Discovery Algorithm Explained: Why Search Drives 70% of Network Traffic

Skool News #47 covers three things at once: a dashboard redesign that consolidates Skool's admin menus, the first real breakdown of how "Skool network" traffic (members the platform itself sends you) actually splits between search, profiles, and browsing, and a set of concrete growth tactics, including a documented 5x jump in YouTube-driven signups from one small change.

## The dashboard gets one home instead of four

Skool merged its separate Metrics, Growth, and Analytics tabs into a single Dashboard, now the default view under Settings. The old Analytics data (total members, MRR, engagement, retention) sits at the top as an overview, with the old Growth-tab charts (about-page visitors, signup sources, signup-over-time, free trial conversion rate, new MRR by source) still available below. Tabs, Categories, and Rules were similarly folded into one "Community" settings tab. The hosts flag one number people misread: the "engagement" percentage is not how many members were active anywhere on Skool, it's the share who visited *your specific group* in the last 30 days. For paid communities, they note a strong correlation between that engagement number and retention, which tracks: members who stop showing up tend to be the ones who cancel.

## Where Skool-network members actually come from: 70/20/10

Roughly 30% of memberships platform-wide originate from Skool's own network rather than external traffic the owner drove. Inside that 30%, the split is: 70% Discovery search, 20% user profiles, and 10% browsing the Discovery homepage. Search dominating matters most for smaller communities, since you don't need a strong overall Discovery rank to surface for a specific keyword. The video's example is a crochet community with 708 members where roughly 94% came from Skool network despite a modest rank, because "crochet" is a low-competition search term. Profile traffic (20%) comes from people viewing another member's profile, seeing what groups they belong to, and joining from there. Skool profiles also rank well on Google for people's names, feeding this indirectly. Pure homepage browsing is the smallest source and mostly benefits already-large communities that show up on the front page.

## What actually moves your Discovery rank

Under Settings > Discovery, Skool now shows your numeric rank plus the factors behind it: recent growth and recent engagement are the core inputs, adjusted by boosts (strong artwork, a well-written about page, a distinctive niche, genuine engagement, an active owner) and penalties (bots or fake accounts, spam or low-quality engagement, off-platform payments, poor support). The hosts' shorthand: don't over-optimize for the mechanics, just run a group worth ranking.

One underrated finding: most profile-traffic (that 20% above) comes from comments, not posts, because a comment is a direct reply someone actually reads, while the average post gets skimmed. The stated advice for new members joining any community is to comment first and post later, both to learn the culture and because posting to self-promote too early is a fast way to get banned.

## The tactic that reportedly 5x'd one creator's traffic

The headline case study: a YouTube creator moved his verbal community pitch from the end of his videos to roughly the 45-second mark, keeping the same content, same subscriber count, same view count. Skool's own traffic-source data showed his YouTube-driven signups jump roughly 5x after the move. The broader takeaway covers layered placement, link in bio, link in the video description (which reportedly pulls more than bio alone), a pinned comment, and a spoken mention early in the video, plus a tip to bulk-edit the description across an entire back catalog via YouTube Studio's "select all > edit description" flow. Similar patterns show up elsewhere: one Instagram creator reportedly built a community past $1.5 million using a comment-a-keyword-to-DM automation instead of a bio link; a TikTok creator won a recent Skool Games cycle by going live daily and inviting viewers directly; and one owner drove signups with QR-code posters around his hometown.
