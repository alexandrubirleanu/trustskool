# Skool's 2026 Roadmap, Straight From CEO Sam Ovens: Traffic Sources, Local Currency, and Localized Discovery

This is a recording from Skool's Q3 2025 Skool Games event: a long, unscripted fireside conversation between Skool co-founder and CEO Sam Ovens and Alex Hormozi, fresh off his own Skool community launch. Buried inside two hours of business advice is a genuine roadmap reveal, straight from the person who runs the platform.

## The 2026 plan, in Sam Ovens' own words

Asked directly what's next for Skool, Ovens said the company had "shipped so many features" that 2026 would start with a deliberate pause: roughly 90 days spent purely on polish rather than new releases. Specific target: notifications. Ovens said Skool currently sends too many, and the plan is to strip out the low-signal ones and only reintroduce alerts that actually matter.

After that polish window, three things are coming:

- **Traffic Sources**, arriving early 2026, shows community owners exactly which channel their members came from (Instagram, YouTube, and so on) and how well each one converts, plus what share is coming organically from Skool's own network. Ovens used a live example from a French-speaking owner in the room, whose community pulls 6% of members from Skool's network despite running entirely in French, evidence, he said, that the organic-network effect is usually bigger than owners assume.
- **Internationalization**: full language support (including right-to-left languages) and local currency pricing. The plan is for a visitor's About page to render in their own language and currency, say, euros and French, regardless of what language the owner built the page in, while the owner still gets paid out in USD.
- **Localized Discovery**, which Ovens called his single focus for the entire year: rather than one English-language Discovery feed, each language gets its own, so a French visitor sees French communities surfaced to them. He argued this alone should push community discovery well past that 6% baseline for non-English creators.

## Why the About page isn't just cosmetic

Later in the conversation, Ovens made a case for using Skool's native About page over an external landing page that goes beyond aesthetics: Skool can only attribute a signup to a traffic source if it happens on their page, and the platform's own conversion data keeps improving as more members already have a Skool account and saved card on file. He cited an average About page conversion rate of roughly 4%, against a general ecommerce benchmark of 1-2%, largely credited to the roughly 30% of members who arrive already logged in through Skool's own network.

## Hormozi's answer on the most underutilized feature

When a founder in the room asked what's most underused on Skool, Hormozi didn't point to anything new: scheduled, recorded calls. Putting calls on a visible schedule and posting the replays, he said, is simple and rare, and the recordings alone reliably pull in roughly 10 times the views of the live session. His broader point: a plain About page, a clear price, a basic classroom, and a consistent call schedule outperforms most attempts at "fancy" setups.

## One open trade-off worth flagging

A founder running a large free AI community asked whether to keep it public or private. Ovens' answer had no clean winner: public groups get indexed by Google and read by LLMs, which can drive more top-of-funnel traffic and let prospects preview content before joining, but private groups convert better precisely because joining is the only way to see anything.

## Why this matters if you're evaluating a Skool community

None of this changes what a community is worth today, but it's a useful lens: platform-level shifts like localized Discovery and currency-aware pricing will likely favor communities outside the English-speaking, USD-priced default over time, and one-click, no-sales-team purchasing is becoming the norm Ovens is actively engineering the platform toward, not a quirk of any one seller.
