# Skool Growth Boost Results: Real Case Studies, New Traffic Data, and the Packaging Problem

Skool News #61 checks in on Growth Boost, the feature where Skool runs off-site ads on Meta to bring new members to eligible communities, now that it's had a few weeks in the wild. The episode walks through real wins across very different niches, a new free reporting tool, and the "packaging" fixes Skool says separate the groups it's converting well from the ones it isn't.

## Still openly labeled "an experiment"

The hosts are refreshingly upfront that Growth Boost isn't a finished, proven system. They describe it plainly as an experiment that might not work, and say Skool is currently paying more to run the ads than it's making back, essentially funding customer acquisition for community owners out of its own pocket while it tunes the targeting. Their framing was direct: "we're just getting started, the best is yet to come." One data point they shared cuts the other way though: roughly 140 groups have already turned Growth Boost off, and most of those have $0 in monthly recurring revenue, suggesting the opt-outs are concentrated among communities that weren't set up to convert yet, not evidence the feature is broadly failing.

## A quieter, genuinely useful addition: traffic sources on the Hobby plan

Alongside Growth Boost news, the episode covers a reporting feature that used to sit behind higher-tier plans and is now available on Skool's cheapest Hobby tier: a breakdown of where a group's About Page visitors actually come from. Demoed live on a small test group, the report showed 65 visitors over 30 days split roughly 56% from Skool's internal network, 43% direct, and a sliver from Google. For anyone running ads or relying on Growth Boost, that breakdown is the difference between guessing and knowing whether new traffic is coming from Skool itself or somewhere else entirely.

## The wins span far beyond "make money" niches

The case studies the hosts pulled together push back on the idea that Growth Boost only helps AI or business coaching groups. Examples referenced included a dance school, a group in the pressure-washing niche that doubled its traffic, and separate communities that picked up 5 and 15 new paying customers respectively. The standout story came from a Skool owner's own community post: after holding off on ads for weeks so her community felt more built out first, she turned Growth Boost on and got 50 new signups and three paying members within 24 hours. Zooming out, she'd gone from two years of minimal traction on social media to roughly 550 members and about $500 in monthly recurring revenue in five months once she leaned into Skool's own discovery and ad tools.

## Why the hosts keep saying "packaging" is the real lever

Because Growth Boost ads and Discovery placements pull directly from a group's existing cover image, group card, and About Page, weak assets there mean weak ad performance no matter how much Skool spends. That's not just a host talking point: in the episode's own comments, one owner said Skool's ad pulled her About Page banner text about fitness rather than the copy that actually describes her community, and another described the current ad creative as feeling disjointed and randomly assembled. Skool's ask to owners, repeated a few times in the episode, is to treat cover image, card, and About Page copy as the thing to fix first, before assuming the algorithm or ad spend is the problem.

## What this means if you're sizing up a community

A platform-funded ad channel baked directly into Skool makes a fast-growing member count even less reliable as a stand-alone signal. A spike could be real demand, or it could be Growth Boost's ad spend converting well for a few weeks. That's exactly why tracking growth over time, not a single snapshot, matters more now than before.
