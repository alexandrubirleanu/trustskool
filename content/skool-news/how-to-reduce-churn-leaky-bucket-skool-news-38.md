# How to Reduce Churn on Skool: The Leaky Bucket Framework from Skool News #38

Episode 38 of Skool News is a nearly hour-long deep dive into member churn: what it actually is, why some churn is fine, and the two levers every paid community has for fighting it, before the hosts widen out into a broader philosophy of running a community day to day.

## Churn is a leaky bucket, not a single scary number

The episode opens with a simple visual the hosts return to throughout: a bucket with a faucet pouring water in labeled "new members," the water level itself labeled "MRR," and water leaking out the bottom labeled "churned members." The point is that churn isn't a standalone metric to panic over, it's one side of a flow. As the hosts put it on screen, the higher your churn, the more that bucket leaks, no matter how much new water you're pouring in.

That framing sets up the episode's core question, addressed directly in the "How to reduce churn" chapter: do you fix the leaks, or add more water? The hosts' answer is that you can "plug the holes or just pour more water in," and both approaches genuinely work, they just solve different problems. Plugging leaks (improving retention) compounds over time and lowers the new-member volume you need just to stand still. Pouring in more water (acquisition) can mask a leaky bucket in the short term, but it's a treadmill that gets more expensive the worse retention gets.

## Why the first few months decide everything

Before getting into tactics, the episode spends time on what "good churn" looks like and on the relationship between member tenure and churn risk. The framing is that the longer a member has already stuck around, the less likely they are to cancel next month, meaning the real churn battle for most communities is won or lost in the earliest weeks after signup, not later. That's also where the episode's tactical section on reducing churn lives, followed by a chapter reviewing real examples of communities that turned their churn numbers around.

## Running a community is gardening, not machine-building

Roughly halfway through, the episode shifts from churn mechanics to a broader argument about community management: that a healthy Skool group behaves more like a garden than a factory line. That framing carries into two more practical chapters toward the end: how to deal with difficult or disruptive members without letting them poison a community's culture, and when it actually makes sense to bring on moderators, plus how to find people worth trusting with that role. Taken together, the back half of the episode makes the case that churn isn't purely a pricing or onboarding problem, it's downstream of whether the community itself is a place worth staying in.

## Why this matters if you're evaluating or running a Skool community

For anyone deciding whether to join a paid Skool community, or already running one, the leaky bucket framing is a useful gut check: a group that's constantly launching to replace churned members is solving a different problem than one that's quietly compounding retention month over month. The second kind is the one worth betting on, whether you're a member picking where to spend money or an admin deciding where to spend your next hour of work.
