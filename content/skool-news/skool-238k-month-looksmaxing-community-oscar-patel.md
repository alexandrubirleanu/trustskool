# The 20-Year-Old Making $238,000/Month Teaching Looksmaxing on Skool

Skool News #48 spotlights Oscar Patel, a 20-year-old whose $39/month looksmaxing community is pulling in an estimated $237,000-$238,000 a month, alongside a new notification-filtering feature and a look at Skool's "quality first" roadmap for 2026.

## Inside the $238K/month looksmaxing community

Oscar's community teaches "looksmaxing," the trending idea that you can improve how you look primarily by getting healthier, and it's built around roughly 6,000 members paying $39 a month. At that price and member count, the hosts put his revenue at around $237,000 a month from a single Skool group. What stands out isn't just the number, it's how evenly split his traffic is: Instagram drives about 27% of it, YouTube about 24%, and Skool itself about 21%, with the rest coming from smaller channels. The hosts note that 21% of a community pulling in around $200K/month works out to roughly $40,000/month in revenue that Skool's own discovery and search traffic is generating for him directly, on top of whatever he's doing on YouTube and Instagram.

His content operation is simple and consistent: a couple of YouTube uploads a week, regular Instagram posts, and a Skool link sitting in both bios. He also runs ManyChat on Instagram, so a comment or DM triggers an automated reply that funnels people toward signing up. There's no complicated funnel behind it, just content on two platforms and one destination link.

## Two more "school of the week" picks

The episode also features Mark McKinnon's Animal Painting School, a $19/month community that teaches, specifically, how to paint animals. It's earning around $5,000/month and the hosts call out how niche and "wholesome" the positioning is. The second is Peter Stone's Ski Academy, pulling in roughly $10,000-$11,000/month. Stone isn't the instructor himself, he partnered with a professional ski coach, Harold Harb, essentially acting as the growth operator while Harb teaches. Their pinned post breaking down the partnership is held up as a template for pairing an expert with someone who can run the business side. The ski community also uses Skool's frame-by-frame native video analysis tool, letting the coach review members' ski footage frame by frame on live calls, a feature also getting use from a Rubik's Cube-solving community and a skateboarding group.

## What's new: notification filters and a "quality first" push

On the feature side, Skool shipped filtering for notifications: you can now narrow your notification feed to just mentions, just comments, just membership requests, or just your call recordings, with a "following" filter for people you follow coming soon. A "just this group" filter, removed earlier for being seen as low-value, got reinstated within 24 hours after users pushed back, which the hosts frame as a broader lesson: you often don't find out what people actually value until you take it away.

Beyond that one feature, the team described 2026 as a "chores before fun" year focused on polish rather than new features: continued work on AutoMod to cut spam, a new call-quality feedback prompt after live calls so Skool can track and fix issues by device, and an effort to reduce the overall volume of notifications and emails sent, especially for members active across many groups. Page load speed is next, followed by what the team called the year's main focus, discovery and search improvements aimed at helping owners get discovered and get more members, with localized languages and currencies further down the roadmap.

## Why this matters if you're sizing up a community

Oscar's numbers are a useful data point precisely because they're not hype, they're a real revenue estimate built from a visible member count, a public price, and a traffic breakdown Skool itself surfaces. That's the exact kind of signal worth checking before joining or benchmarking against any Skool community, rather than trusting a screenshot of "$200K+/month" with no context behind it.
