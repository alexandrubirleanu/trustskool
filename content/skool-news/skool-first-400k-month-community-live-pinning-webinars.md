# Skool's Race to Its First $400K/Month Community, Plus Live Call Pinning and Webinars on the Way

Skool News #30 covers a live-calls upgrade that just shipped, a roadmap update on subscription tiers and Skool webinars, a community on the verge of becoming the first to hit $400K/month, and a wave of large creators launching communities on the platform.

## Live calls: pin anyone for everyone, not just yourself

Skool's live call rooms have moved past the plain grid view. Hosts can now pin a participant so that everyone on the call sees that spotlighted view, not just a local full-screen toggle. If a co-host is speaking, you can pin them big for the whole room; when someone else needs the floor, unpin and pin the new speaker instead. The feature is described as WYSIWYG, meaning what the host arranges live is exactly what members see in the room and exactly what ends up in the saved recording, so there's no separate editing step after the fact. Individual members can still tap into a full-screen view of anyone locally without affecting what other attendees or the recording show. Right now only one person can be spotlighted at a time (even though multiple people can be "pinned" in the queue), but the team says support for spotlighting multiple people simultaneously, useful for two-person conversations inside a larger call, is coming soon and will also carry through to recordings.

## Roadmap: webinars before tiers, tiers before better analytics

Subscription tiers are "in progress" and getting closer, but the bigger near-term item is Skool webinars, a stripped-down call format for large one-to-many events. Unlike Skool's existing call rooms, webinar attendees join with no camera and no mic by default; they can only watch and use chat unless the host explicitly pulls them onto the stage. This is aimed at the 1,000-2,000+ person event use case where letting everyone unmute would be chaos, the exact reason the hosts note this very episode was still being livestreamed on Zoom rather than Skool. Webinars are estimated to ship in the next 7-14 days, ahead of subscription tiers, which are targeted for sometime in October. A significantly improved analytics dashboard is further out, roughly a month behind tiers, and is described as a jump from "average" straight to best-in-class rather than an incremental update.

## The first $400K/month community, and the Skool Network's role in it

The headline story: a community run by a host named Spencer sits atop Skool's all-time money leaderboard (visible inside the Skoolers community under Games) at $394,000 in monthly recurring revenue, just shy of what would be the first community ever to cross $400K/month on the platform. What's notable for anyone running or evaluating a Skool community is the breakdown of where that revenue comes from: the Skool Network, Skool's built-in discovery and referral system, is credited with driving 25% of it, roughly $100,000/month, for a cost the hosts peg at $99. That ratio is presented as a standout example of what organic, platform-native discovery can do for MRR without separate paid acquisition, on top of whatever a creator is already driving through their own audience.

## Big creators keep choosing Skool, and there's now a dedicated launch manager

The episode also flags several sizable creators who've recently launched communities: a well-known surfer building a spirituality and energy-connection focused community, an English-language coach with roughly 700K followers, and a short-form dance content creator with 18 million subscribers. Skool says it now has a dedicated Customer Success Manager, Mark Novikov, whose job is helping larger creators launch on the platform, and that the company will help qualifying creators launch for free. The bar for "big" is described as being based more on views than raw follower count, with six-figure-plus subscriber counts as a rough benchmark; creators or agencies who fit can reach out directly to mark@skool.com.
