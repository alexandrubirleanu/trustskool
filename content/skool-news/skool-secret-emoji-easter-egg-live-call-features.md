# Skool's Secret Emoji Easter Egg and New Live Call Features, Explained

Skool News #50 covers a batch of live call upgrades: a redesigned emoji reaction set with a hidden Easter egg, picture-in-picture support so you never lose track of a call, an overlay fix for screen sharing, and a new troubleshooting tab for laggy calls. The episode also flags the closing days of the current Skool Games season.

## The emoji redesign, and the secret it's hiding

Skool rebuilt its live call emoji reactions from scratch. The team says they started using the reaction feature internally for their own team meetings (not its original intended use) and quickly decided the old emoji set looked "kind of lame," so they shipped a visually punchier version.

Buried inside the new reaction menu is an Easter egg: there's a hidden text field where typing something in unlocks a set of secret emojis, including a cat and a rocket, that don't appear in the standard picker. The hosts wouldn't confirm the exact trigger, only joking that it "might be three characters... might be 10... it's 12," and left it as a challenge for the community to find. If you run a Skool community with live calls, it's worth having someone poke around the reaction menu to dig it up.

## Picture-in-picture keeps reactions and chat visible

Previously, if you switched browser tabs during a live call, you'd lose visibility into chat and emoji reactions until you switched back. Now, moving away from the call tab triggers an automatic picture-in-picture window showing the call, so hosts and attendees can keep an eye on reactions and chat from another tab.

## No more "infinity mirror" during screen share

Screen sharing had a longstanding annoyance: presenters couldn't see attendee faces, chat, or reactions while sharing, so there was no way to gauge whether the audience was engaged. Skool has fixed this by automatically surfacing chat, reactions, and participant video in an overlay the moment you start sharing your screen, rather than only showing the "infinity mirror" effect of the screen-share window reflecting itself. It's a small change but a meaningful one for anyone running webinars or trainings inside a Skool community.

## A troubleshooting tab and a feedback loop for call issues

Live calls now have a troubleshooting tab (Settings → Troubleshooting) that reports network stability and system load in real time, useful if a call is lagging due to too many open tabs/apps or a weak connection. Skool has also added a post-call rating prompt: after ending a call, you can rate the experience and describe any problem you hit. The team says this feedback has already driven real fixes and encourages community owners to have members submit it whenever calls misbehave.

## Skool Games deadline and a $26K/month case study

The current Skool Games season ends this Saturday at 10am Pacific, with winners earning an all-expenses-paid trip to LA plus a trophy. The next season kicks off April 1st and runs for 90 days. The hosts pulled a few leaderboard highlights worth noting for anyone benchmarking community growth: two brand-new tech-category communities (under four months old) each added over $100,000 in revenue in 90 days, and one is driving most of its traffic from Twitter/X, an unusual channel for Skool growth. They also spotlighted Tristan Spohn's Acting Lab, run by a Stranger Things actor, which charges around $200/month and generates roughly $26,000/month by funneling YouTube viewers into the paid community.

None of this changes how communities are ranked or evaluated from the outside, but it's a useful reminder that call quality and engagement tooling are an active, fast-moving part of the platform, and worth watching if you're choosing or running a Skool community.
