# Dickie Bush, Nicolas Cole and Ship 30 for 30: The Course That Turned a Tweet Into a Skool Community

Ship 30 for 30 is a free Skool community of roughly 4,650 members built around a simple, almost absurdly literal premise: write 30 short essays in 30 days and you'll come out the other side a genuinely better online writer. It's the community layer of a course that has been running since December 2020, and it's run by two writers, Dickie Bush and Nicolas Cole, who each built large audiences by publishing constantly and in public.

## Who are Dickie Bush and Nicolas Cole

Dickie Bush started the whole project almost by accident. According to Ship 30 for 30's own account of its origin, he began writing online in January 2020, found that weekly blog posts gave him too slow a feedback loop, and switched to publishing short, roughly 250-word pieces daily instead. During his own 30-day writing sprint he says he grew his audience by over 500% and made his first dollar online. When he tweeted the idea of a group challenge to his then-500 followers, fifty people joined a Slack channel within six days, and that informal cohort became Ship 30 for 30 ([ship30for30.com](https://www.ship30for30.com/)).

Nicolas Cole brought a decade of prior writing credentials to the partnership. He built one of the first "e-famous" gaming blogs as a teenager, studied creative writing in college, and worked as an entry-level copywriter at a Chicago ad agency before challenging himself to write on Quora every day for a year, a run that made him, by his own account, the most-read writer on the platform out of roughly 200 million users by 2015. He went on to write more than 400 columns for Inc Magazine, ranked among its ten most-read contributors, built a ghostwriting agency, and published "The Art and Business of Online Writing" in 2020 ([ship30for30.com](https://www.ship30for30.com/)).

## What members actually get

The Skool community's own about page describes Ship 30 for 30 as a self-paced curriculum that has taught more than 10,000 students the fundamentals of digital writing, structured around writing 30 "Atomic Essays" at your own pace rather than on a fixed daily cohort schedule. Members get the full course library plus access to the community itself, where hundreds of other writers post drafts, swap feedback, and work through the same prompts Bush and Cole originally built for their own daily practice.

## Why it's grown

What makes Ship 30 for 30 an interesting case is how directly the product mirrors the founders' own method. Bush's entire pitch is that daily, short-form publishing beats infrequent long-form writing for building an audience and a skill at the same time, and the course exists because that's exactly what happened to him personally in 30 days. Cole's decade of high-volume publishing across Quora, Inc, and ghostwriting adds a second, independently-earned data point for the same thesis. Combined audiences reported in the "combined 750,000 followers" range gave the course distribution before it ever needed paid ads, and the shift to a self-paced Skool community in 2024 let new members join anytime rather than waiting for the next cohort.
