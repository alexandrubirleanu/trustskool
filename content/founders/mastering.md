# Rob Mayzes and Mastering.com Members Club: Inside Skool's Largest Paid Music Production Community

Mastering.com Members Club bills itself as the largest paid music production community in the world, and at roughly 37,000 members paying $29 a month, that claim is at least directionally credible for the Skool platform. It's run by Rob Mayzes, a UK-trained audio engineer who spent the better part of a decade building an audience before turning it into a full membership business.

## Who is Rob Mayzes

Rob Mayzes holds a BSc in Audio Production from the University of Kent, where he studied from 2011 to 2014 ([LinkedIn](https://uk.linkedin.com/in/rob-mayzes-44129b86)). He's a working mixing and mastering engineer with more than a decade of experience, and he's also a bassist and producer in his own right. He started teaching music production online in 2015, initially under the brand "Musician on a Mission" before rebuilding the business as Mastering.com. That YouTube channel has grown to roughly 260,000 subscribers, and his mailing list runs to about 370,000 people ([RocketReach profile](https://rocketreach.co/rob-mayzes-email_10987461); [flow.page bio](https://flow.page/rob-mayzes)) — an audience built almost entirely on free tutorial content before the paid community existed.

Beyond Mastering.com, Mayzes is also a co-owner of The Ffelt (a production team), BlackFoxMastering.com, and Lotimus.com, giving him a portfolio of music-industry businesses rather than a single product. He's listed as Founder and CEO on both his LinkedIn and on The Org's organizational chart for the company ([The Org](https://theorg.com/org/mastering-com/org-chart/rob-mayzes)).

## What members actually get

The free tier of the Skool community functions as a hub for events and free courses; the paid $29/month tier unlocks the core curriculum. That includes a structured "Music Production 101" and "201" course sequence, a library of 100+ hours of audio courses, monthly one-on-one Zoom calls with working professionals, guest sessions that have included Grammy-winning engineers and producers, weekly live Q&A and mentorship calls, and a "Sync 101" track specifically about getting music placed in TV, film, ads, and games — a revenue stream most independent producers don't know how to access on their own.

The community is explicitly genre-agnostic and DAW-agnostic, meaning it doesn't assume members use a specific digital audio workstation, which widens its addressable audience considerably compared to software-specific communities.

## Why it's grown

The core growth mechanism is a classic content-to-community funnel executed patiently over roughly a decade. Mayzes built a large, trust-based YouTube audience first, teaching free mixing and mastering fundamentals, and only later layered a recurring paid community on top of that existing relationship. That sequencing matters: by the time the Skool community launched, there was already a 260,000-subscriber, 370,000-person mailing list of people who already believed his teaching worked, which is a very different growth problem than starting a paid community cold.

The specific promise of access to Grammy-winning guests and monthly 1:1 professional calls also gives the community a tangible, recurring reason to stay subscribed beyond the initial course content — something a lot of "course-in-a-community-wrapper" products lack. For an audience of independent, often self-taught musicians who don't have access to industry mentorship through traditional channels (labels, studios, conservatories), that access is the actual product being sold, more than the video library itself.
