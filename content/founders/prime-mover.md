# Russell Brunson's Prime Mover: Inside the ClickFunnels Founder's 36,000-Member Skool

Selling Online / Prime Mover carries a name that doubles as a philosophy: members are pushed to think of themselves as "Prime Movers," the people who envision a new reality and build it, rather than passive consumers of yet another marketing course. Nearly 37,000 people have joined, and Russell Brunson himself posts inside it directly.

## Who is Russell Brunson

Brunson co-founded ClickFunnels with Todd Dickerson in 2014, building it without a single dollar of venture capital, funded instead through his own company, Etison LLC, and an email list of over a million people he'd already built before launch. A Forbes profile from May 2017 covered the company's trajectory in detail, noting it had reached $360 million in annual revenue as an entirely self-funded SaaS business ([Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/robertadams/2017/05/16/this-entrepreneur-built-a-360-million-dollar-saas-business-and-it-was-entirely-self-funded/)), a figure that later independent reporting put at over $170 million a year in the funnel-building product line alone as the company matured ([Founder Reports](https://founderreports.com/interview/clickfunnels/)).

He's also a three-time New York Times bestselling author, with books including "DotCom Secrets" and "Expert Secrets" that predate and helped build the audience ClickFunnels eventually sold to. Brunson has built a following of several million entrepreneurs over roughly two decades of teaching sales funnels and direct-response marketing, and he posts and interacts inside the Prime Mover Skool community under his own account, rather than delegating it entirely to a team ([Skool profile](https://www.skool.com/@russellbrunson)).

## What members actually get

The community centers on what Brunson calls one-to-many selling, frameworks for presenting an offer to a large audience at once rather than relying on one-to-one sales conversations. Members share session notes from Brunson's live events, including his Funnel Hacking Live conference, post wins from applying specific frameworks, and discuss offer creation, affiliate marketing, and funnel building as a connected system rather than separate skills.

The tone throughout the group's guidelines is deliberately high-standard: strict rules against spam, and messaging that frames membership as something to live up to rather than a passive subscription.

## Why it's grown

Prime Mover's size is inseparable from Brunson's decades of pre-existing audience-building outside of Skool. He built his following through books, live events, and ClickFunnels itself long before the Skool community existed, which means the group functions less as an acquisition channel and more as a retention and community layer for an audience that already trusts him.

That's a different growth pattern than most large Skool communities, which tend to be built primarily through short-form content aimed at cold audiences. Prime Mover instead behaves like a reunion space for people who already bought into Brunson's broader body of work, which explains both its size and the intensity of engagement inside it.
