# Quincy Perkins and iPhone Cinematics: A Sundance-Credited Filmmaker's Skool Community

iPhone Cinematics is a free Skool community of around 2,500 members built around a specific promise: turning a phone in your pocket into a genuine cinema camera. The person teaching that skill isn't a phone-filmmaking influencer with no film background, he's a working filmmaker with real festival credits and two decades in the industry.

## Who is Quincy Perkins

Perkins was born and raised in Key West, Florida, and got his start in the industry as a line producer and filmmaker on projects for National Geographic, including a two-year assignment studying the Galapagos Islands ecosystem ([Screen Heat Miami bio](https://www.screenheatmiami.com/quincy-perkins)). Over more than 20 years, his bio credits him with work on both independent films and larger productions including *Master and Commander*, *Men in Black*, *Sweet Home Alabama*, and *The Curious Case of Benjamin Button*.

His own directorial short films, including *The Messenger: Based on a True Story* and *The Hemp Car*, have collectively won more than 20 awards and screened at Sundance, Cannes, Telluride, and other major festivals, per that same bio. His feature documentary *The Little Firemen* is available on Amazon Prime, and an earlier documentary, *Underwater: The Making of the Key West Nutcracker*, won Best Florida Documentary in 2007. He's also co-founder and president of the Key West Film Festival, and his bio notes he was invited to work on set with director Peter Weir and actor Russell Crowe on one of cinema's more expensive productions.

## What members actually get

The Skool community offers free resources for filmmakers shooting on iPhone, including a free Apple Film Look LUT (a color-grading preset designed to mimic a more cinematic color profile), downloadable resources, and video tutorials housed in the community's Classroom tab. The framing throughout is explicit about the emotional side of the craft too: learning to shoot cinematically on a phone as a solo creator can be an isolating process, and the community is positioned as the antidote to that, connecting members with others on the same path rather than just handing out technical tips in isolation.

Perkins runs several related filmmaking-focused Skool communities beyond this one, covering adjacent topics like cinematography, screenwriting, and film distribution, suggesting iPhone Cinematics is one piece of a broader ecosystem he's built for independent and mobile filmmakers.

## Why it's grown

The credibility gap between Perkins and most "shoot cinematic video on your phone" content online is significant: he isn't reverse-engineering filmmaking language from YouTube tutorials, he's applying two decades of actual Hollywood and documentary production experience, including real festival wins, to the constraint of a phone camera. That gives his tips on framing, lighting, and color grading a grounding that's hard for a purely social-media-native creator to match.

Free tools like the Apple Film Look LUT also function as a low-friction entry point, letting filmmakers get an immediate, tangible upgrade to their footage before deciding whether to go deeper into his paid offerings or his other filmmaking communities.
