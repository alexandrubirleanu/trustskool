# Lawrence Johnson and Sprint University: Inside Skool's 7,200-Member Speed Training Community

Sprint University is a free Skool community with about 7,240 members built around one focused promise: get faster, using the same system its founder uses to train competitive sprinters. Unlike the countless general "fitness" groups on Skool, this one is run by someone with an active, documented competitive track record of his own.

## Who is Lawrence Johnson

Lawrence "L.O." Johnson is a professional track and field athlete and a six-time NCAA All-American, according to his LinkedIn profile, which lists him as a pro sprinter and the founder of Sprint University ([LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/lawrence-johnson-59b144226/)). His competitive career included a stint at the University of Wisconsin, where he's listed on the men's track and field roster and set school records before closing out his collegiate career at the 2023 NCAA Outdoor Championships ([Wisconsin Badgers roster](https://uwbadgers.com/sports/mens-track-and-field/roster/lawrence--johnson/11718)).

His own coaching site describes him building Sprint University's "Pillars of Speed System" after years spent competing at the highest level and coaching athletes one-on-one, citing appearances at Continental Tour Gold and Silver competitions ([Sprint University](https://thesprintuniversity.com/home)). The Skool community's about page repeats the same biography, describing him as a "current Professional sprinter, multiple-time NCAA All-American, and former Division 1 athlete" ([Sprint University on Skool](https://www.skool.com/sprintuniversity/about)).

## What members actually get

Sprint University is free to join and centers on the Pillars of Speed System, which the community describes as covering movement mastery, recovery, nutrition, and mindset training for sprinters. Johnson positions the free Skool group as running the same methodology he uses in his own training and in coaching athletes directly, spanning youth, collegiate, masters, and elite-level sprinters.

That's a meaningfully different pitch than a generic speed and agility program: it's built by someone still actively competing and coaching at a high level, rather than a coach who competed once, years ago, and has since pivoted entirely to content. The gap between "I used to sprint" and "I'm still racing at Continental Tour meets" matters to an audience of serious sprinters evaluating who to trust with their training.

## Why it's grown

At 7,240 members and no cost to join, Sprint University benefits from a credibility structure that's hard to fake in a niche sport: verifiable college results, a named university roster listing, and ongoing competitive appearances that anyone can check against meet results. Track and field is a small, tightly networked world, and claims about NCAA All-American status or competing at Continental Tour meets are easy for other sprinters and coaches to verify or debunk.

That transparency likely does a lot of the community's growth work on its own. A sprinter deciding whether to trust a training system doesn't need to take Lawrence Johnson's word for his credentials; his Wisconsin roster page and his NCAA record are sitting in public view, which is a rarer thing in the online fitness-coaching space than it should be.
