# Mikey Caloca and Mikey's Money House: Inside Skool's 7,700-Member Personal Finance Community

Mikey's Money House is a 7,771-member Skool community focused on practical personal finance: fixing and building credit, saving, paying off debt, and starting side hustles. It costs $8/month, one of the more affordable finance communities on Skool [Skool](https://www.skool.com/mikeysmoneyhouse/about).

## Who runs it

The community is led by Mikey Caloca, a personal-finance content creator who built his following on TikTok. Caloca joined TikTok in December 2024 and has grown to over 1.1 million followers and 19.5 million likes on the platform, documenting his own finances — taxes, credit cards, student loan payoff — with self-deprecating humor rather than polished guru positioning [TikTok](https://www.tiktok.com/@mikeycaloca). He's based in Kennesaw, Georgia, and has built his audience largely by being transparent about his own numbers in real time, including publicly tracking his savings toward a $200,000 first-home goal (having reached roughly $117,279 at last public update). His LinkedIn profile lists over 1.2 million followers across his social accounts [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/mikey-caloca-3844a51a2/).

## What members actually get

Mikey's Money House runs three live classes per week on rotating personal-finance topics, backed by a library of roughly 75 hours of recorded past sessions members can work through at their own pace, per the community's own description. The group also runs monthly giveaways worth over $500 and offers members the option to book one-on-one time with Caloca directly — a level of individual access that's unusual at this community size and price point. The subject matter skews toward the exact problems a Gen Z or young millennial audience is dealing with in real time: credit repair, first-time saving systems, debt payoff strategy, and side-income ideas, rather than long-term investing theory.

## Why it's grown

Caloca's TikTok-native, transparent style is the clear engine behind this community's growth. Unlike finance educators who present a finished, wealthy persona, Caloca built his audience by showing his own financial journey unfinished and in public — a documented savings goal, real numbers, real setbacks — which builds a different kind of trust with younger audiences who are skeptical of traditional finance-guru marketing. That same transparency carries into the Skool community: it's positioned explicitly as "a family that grows, learns, and wins together" rather than a static course library, and the combination of live classes plus 1:1 access at an $8/month price point gives it an unusually high perceived value for what members are actually paying.

The one thing prospective members should note is the no-refunds policy the community lists on its about page — worth reading before joining, as with any paid Skool community.
