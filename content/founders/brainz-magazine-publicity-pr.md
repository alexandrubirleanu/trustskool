# Caroline Winkvist and Brainz Magazine: Inside Skool's 6,600-Member Publicity Community

Brainz Magazine Publicity & PR is a free Skool community of about 6,608 members, built as the membership layer around an actual publication rather than a solo creator's course. It exists to funnel members toward publicity opportunities, media coverage, and consideration for the magazine's award lists, and behind it sits a real, registered media company with a named founder.

## Who is Caroline Winkvist

Brainz Magazine is a global digital business and lifestyle publication based in Stockholm, Sweden, founded in 2019 by Caroline Winkvist, who serves as CEO and Editor-in-Chief ([Brainz Magazine team page](https://www.brainzmagazine.com/team)). The magazine operates under Winkvist Group AB and covers entrepreneurship, leadership, and personal development through contributor interviews and articles ([Brainz Magazine about page](https://www.brainzmagazine.com/about)). Company-profile data aggregator Tracxn also lists Brainz Magazine as a Stockholm-based media company with Winkvist at the helm ([Tracxn company profile](https://tracxn.com/d/companies/brainz-magazine/__e0oERZPiSx4-11I4LTMOcgt6vAUDLeTwbgk9phb1rHs)).

Unlike most Skool communities built around a single personal brand, Brainz Magazine's Skool presence is an extension of an existing publishing business with its own editorial team, meaning the "founder" here is closer to a magazine CEO than a solo course creator.

## What members actually get

The community is free to join and is explicitly framed around access rather than education: members can connect with administrators carrying the internal title "Selection Committee Brainz Magazine" to discuss Executive Contributor Partnerships, which are Brainz's paid guaranteed-publicity packages, or to explore consideration for the magazine's various award lists. It functions less like a course and more like a gateway into the magazine's existing PR and publicity business, giving members a direct line to the people who decide what gets published and who gets awarded.

That structure makes sense for a company whose core paid product, contributor and publicity placements, already exists independent of Skool. The community isn't teaching a skill from scratch; it's organizing access to a real editorial and publicity operation that predates it.

## Why it's grown

Brainz Magazine's Skool community has an advantage most creator-led groups don't: an actual media brand with years of published content and real award programs standing behind it. Entrepreneurs and executives looking for press coverage or executive-contributor status have a concrete reason to join a free community that puts them in direct contact with the people running that program, rather than hoping an anonymous algorithm surfaces their pitch.

At 6,608 members, the group also benefits from Brainz Magazine's existing reach as a publication with its own contributor network and audience built over several years, meaning the Skool community likely absorbs interest that already exists around the magazine itself, rather than having to generate that interest from zero.
