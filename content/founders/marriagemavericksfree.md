# Josh and Cassie Fuller and Marriage Mavericks Free: Inside Skool's 2,600-Member Marriage Community

Marriage Mavericks Free makes a pointed promise: save your marriage even if your spouse isn't on board yet. The free Skool community built around that pitch has reached roughly 2,637 members.

## Who runs it

The community is led by married couple Josh and Cassie Fuller, whose own site describes a marriage that nearly ended after years of stress, demanding careers, and breakdowns in communication, a crisis serious enough that a near-fatal car accident became a turning point that forced them to prioritize the relationship ([Marriage Mavericks](https://marriagemavericks.com/)). They say they tried therapists, books, and workshops without lasting success before working out their own approach over what they describe as 18 years spent refining it with hundreds of couples, including couples where one spouse was already talking about divorce. They host the Marriage Mavericks podcast together, available on Spotify under both their names ([Spotify](https://open.spotify.com/show/57a1ObnQ5ulNdd2qSGntV7)), maintain an active YouTube channel and Facebook page under the Marriage Mavericks name ([YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/@marriagemavericks); [Facebook](https://www.facebook.com/marriagemavericks/)), and have accumulated customer reviews on Trustpilot tied to their business rather than only to their own marketing ([Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/marriagemavericks.com)).

## What members actually get

The couple's own materials describe their client base as skewing toward severe situations: couples dealing with affairs, separation, or active divorce conversations, with the Fullers stating that a large share of the people they work with are trying to save a marriage where the other spouse has already given up on it. The free Skool tier functions as an entry point into that broader practice, likely built to demonstrate their approach before steering serious cases toward paid coaching.

## Why it's grown

Most relationship content online assumes both partners are willing participants in fixing things, which leaves a real gap for the specific and often more painful situation of one partner trying to save a marriage alone. The Fullers' own documented crisis, a near-fatal accident forcing a reckoning after years of failed attempts at fixing things through conventional means, gives their story a specificity that generic relationship-coaching marketing usually lacks, and having it corroborated by an independent podcast, a Trustpilot review history, and years of YouTube content rather than resting only on the Skool page's own copy adds a layer of accountability. A free community removes the cost barrier at the exact moment someone is deciding whether their marriage is even salvageable, which is a more sensitive purchase decision than most niches on Skool involve.
