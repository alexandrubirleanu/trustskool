# Curtis Fry, Cheech Pierce, and Fly Fish Food Academy: Inside Skool's Fly Tying Community

With close to 3,400 members, Fly Fish Food Academy promises to teach people to tie, cast, and fish "like a pro" through structured classes rather than scattered YouTube tutorials. It's the education arm of Fly Fish Food, a fly tying materials and content company founded by Curtis Fry and Clark "Cheech" Pierce.

## Who runs it

Fry and Pierce started Fly Fish Food back in 2012 as a simple blog documenting the recipes for the flies they were tying, mostly so they wouldn't forget their own patterns ([Fly Fish Food About Us](https://www.flyfishfood.com/pages/about-us)). Video tutorials followed the blog, and demand for the materials shown in those videos followed the videos, so the pair pooled around $300 for their first small Hareline order and grew the operation from Fry's basement, to his garage, to a cramped 1,100-square-foot shop by 2016. In a feature interview with Voyage Utah Magazine, Fry walked through that slow, self-funded build-out in detail, describing the years of unpaid work that went into turning a hobby blog into a real materials and education business ([Voyage Utah Magazine interview](https://voyageutah.com/interview/inspiring-conversations-with-curtis-fry-of-fly-fish-food/)).

That history matters for how the Skool community is positioned: it isn't a coaching program built by someone monetizing an audience after the fact, it's the newest layer on top of a fly fishing brand that's been publishing tutorials and selling tying materials for over a decade.

## What members actually get

Fly Fish Food Academy is pitched as an exclusive space for organized learning, real-time feedback, and direct access to Fry, Pierce, and the other Fly Fish Food team, alongside peer networking with other tiers. The pitch is explicitly built around removing ego from the room, describing an environment as welcoming to someone who "bought his first vise" last week as to a thirty-year tying veteran.

That structure sits on top of Fly Fish Food's existing content library and materials shop, so members arriving through Skool are typically already familiar with the brand from its blog, YouTube channel, or the tying kits and materials it sells directly ([Fly Fish Food Academy page](https://www.flyfishfood.com/pages/fly-fish-food-academy)).

## Why it's grown

The advantage Fly Fish Food had going into Skool is the same one that's carried the company since 2012: a genuinely long track record of free, useful tutorial content that built trust with fly tiers long before there was anything to buy. By the time Fly Fish Food Academy launched, Fry and Pierce weren't introducing themselves to a cold audience, they were offering a more structured, interactive version of something people already watched and trusted.

Fly tying and fly fishing are also hobbies with a strong apprenticeship tradition, people already expect to learn from someone more experienced, which makes a paid, structured community a natural next step for a brand that's spent over a decade building exactly that kind of teacher-student relationship with its audience.
