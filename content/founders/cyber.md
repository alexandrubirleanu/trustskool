# Sarvesh Murali and MyFirstHack: The 93,000-Member Path Into Cybersecurity

MyFirstHack bills itself as the biggest cybersecurity community on Skool, and at over 93,000 members for $9 a month, that claim is hard to argue with. The pitch is narrow and practical: free courses, mentorship, networking, and a structured route into a first cybersecurity job, aimed squarely at people with no degree and no prior experience in the field.

## Who is Sarvesh Murali

The community is built and run by Sarvesh Murali, who is based in Chennai, Tamil Nadu, India, and studied at the University of Madras according to his LinkedIn profile ([LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/sarvesh-murali-62a59822b/)). The official MyFirstHack site credits him directly in its footer as the creator of the platform ([myfirsthack.com](https://myfirsthack.com/)). He runs the brand's YouTube channel under his own name, MyFirstHack | Sarvesh Murali ([YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/@myfirsthackofficial)), and posts regularly on Instagram both through the main MyFirstHack account and a separate CyberDojo account with roughly 34,000 followers ([@myfirsthack](https://www.instagram.com/myfirsthack/), [@cyberdojoofficial](https://www.instagram.com/cyberdojoofficial/)).

Murali positions himself as a cybersecurity consultant and mentor rather than a credentialed academic, and his content leans on that identity: short videos aimed at people who feel locked out of the industry by degree requirements or gatekeeping, paired with a longer structured curriculum inside the paid community.

## What members actually get

Inside MyFirstHack, the core offering is a 90-day, hands-on program built around a "zero to first job" roadmap. That includes a cybersecurity roadmap for total beginners, Security+ certification preparation, portfolio-building project tutorials, and tools aimed at the job application process itself, not just the technical skills. The 90-day framing is intentional: Murali has been explicit in his content that the timeline exists because real foundational skill, not surface-level familiarity, takes that long to build.

At $9 a month, the price point is low enough to function as a volume play rather than a premium credential, which fits the "free courses, mentorship, networking" framing in the community's own description. The paid tier appears to unlock structured progression and closer access rather than gatekeeping basic content behind a paywall.

## Why it's grown

MyFirstHack's growth traces back to a genuinely underserved gap: cybersecurity has a well-documented hiring problem where entry-level roles routinely ask for years of experience or certifications that beginners have no clear path to obtaining. Murali's content, distributed across TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube under the same brand and same face, gives prospective members a low-friction way to sample his teaching style before committing even $9 a month.

The multi-platform consistency matters here. Rather than treating Skool as a standalone product, MyFirstHack functions as the natural next step for anyone who has already been watching Sarvesh Murali's short-form content, which keeps acquisition cost low and trust high by the time someone actually joins. That's a pattern that shows up across a lot of large, low-price Skool communities: the platform isn't where the audience gets built, it's where an already-warm audience gets organized.
