# Grant Anderson and Kettlebell Transformation: Inside Skool's 2,700-Member Free Fitness Community

Kettlebell Transformation Free teaches men to build strength at home using kettlebells, and it has grown to roughly 2,737 members without charging for access.

## Who runs it

The community traces back to Grant Anderson, who lists himself on LinkedIn as co-founder of Kettlebell Transformation ([LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/grant-anderson-260437233/)). Anderson and a business partner named Sean previously co-owned Chicago Primal Gym, a real gym with its own LinkedIn company page, which Sean started in his backyard after leaving a corporate finance job in 2013 and grew to more than 350 members before the pandemic ([LinkedIn: Chicago Primal Gym](https://www.linkedin.com/company/chicago-primal-gym)). Anderson is the more public-facing half of the operation, with an Instagram following of more than 140,000 under the handle @kettlebellgrant built around kettlebell training content. After running Chicago Primal Gym together for years, the pair moved their kettlebell programming online, which is what now operates as Kettlebell Transformation.

## What members actually get

The free Skool community's stated goal is simple: help men transform their strength and fitness from home using kettlebells rather than a commercial gym, positioned as a fast, efficient alternative to a full gym membership. It functions as a top-of-funnel entry point into the pair's broader Kettlebell Transformation brand, which also runs a paid Kettlebell Academy subscription with a 6-level mastery roadmap and a dedicated Kettlebell Swing tracking app listed on the Apple App Store. The free tier itself is built around simple starter instructions, a community of men holding each other accountable, and a shorter masterclass meant to prove out the training style before anyone commits to the paid programs.

## Why it's grown

Kettlebell training occupies a specific niche inside home fitness: it requires one piece of equipment, takes up little space, and has built-in credibility with people who've tried and abandoned generic bodyweight or resistance-band programs. Anderson and Sean's years running an actual physical gym together, rather than starting as online-only content creators, gives the brand an operating history that predates its social media presence, which is a different credibility path than most fitness creators who build an audience first and open a gym (if ever) second. A free community removes the friction of committing money to a niche training method before someone has tried it, while giving Anderson's existing 140k-plus Instagram audience an obvious next step to convert into.
