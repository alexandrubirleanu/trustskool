# Skool Affiliate Program & Referrals: Every Question Answered

Skool runs two separate affiliate systems that people constantly conflate: a platform-level program that pays you for referring people to Skool itself, and a community-level tool that lets any group owner turn their own members into paid referrers. Below is a direct answer to every common question about how both work, what breaks, and whether the incentive structure is as sketchy as critics claim.

## How does Skool's own affiliate program work, and what commission rate does it pay?

Skool pays **40% of a referred customer's monthly subscription, recurring for as long as they stay subscribed** — not a one-time bounty. Since Pro plan pricing is $99/month, that works out to roughly $39.60/month per active paying referral; on the $9/month Hobby plan it's closer to $3.60/month ([Skool Pricing](https://www.skool.com/pricing), [Skool Affiliate Program](https://www.skool.com/affiliate-program)). Attribution runs on a 14-day last-touch cookie: whoever's link the person clicked *most recently* before signing up within that 14-day window gets the credit ([Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/187-how-to-setup-affiliate)). One structurally important detail: if someone in your referred network later creates their own paid Skool community, that referral is automatically attributed back up to you too — a passive-income mechanic that's also the basis of the pyramid-scheme criticism covered below. Commissions are held roughly 14 days before becoming payable (refund protection), paid out to a linked bank account, and you must be 18+ to withdraw.

## How do you set up member affiliates so your own community members can earn commissions for referrals?

This is a **Pro plan-only feature** ($99/month tier) — it isn't available on the $9/month Hobby plan ([Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/192-how-to-set-up-member-referrals-for-your-community)). To enable it: go to your profile → Settings → the Admin gear icon for your community → the **Affiliates** tab, then set a commission percentage (commonly cited range is 10-50%). Once turned on, every member automatically gets their own unique referral link inside the community to share. A few rules govern it: group owners and admins are explicitly excluded from earning on their own group — only regular members can be affiliates; commissions trigger only on genuinely new member signups (someone who leaves and rejoins with your link doesn't regenerate a payout); and you can change the commission percentage at any time, but Skool's own docs warn that lowering it "may upset your affiliates and damage your reputation," since the change applies retroactively to existing affiliates too.

## What do you do if a referral wasn't attributed correctly?

First, check the five documented reasons Skool attribution silently fails before assuming it's a bug ([Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/187-how-to-setup-affiliate)):

1. **Inactive membership at signup time** — the referrer must have had an active membership in the group at the exact moment the new member joined.
2. **Last-touch override** — if the new member clicked a *different* affiliate link more recently than yours within the attribution window, that link wins, not the first one they clicked.
3. **Ownership change** — if the community's ownership transferred after you referred someone, the affiliate credit is cancelled.
4. **Archived group** — if the owner archives the community, all affiliate commissions on it stop.
5. **Rejoin, not a new join** — someone leaving and re-entering via your link doesn't count as a new referral.

Skool's system is fully automated end-to-end, and Skool explicitly asks affiliates not to contact support to request manual attribution changes or backdated fixes. Practically: work through the checklist above first. If none of it explains the gap and you genuinely believe it's a tracking bug (link not firing, cookie not set), support is the last resort, not the first move, and expect them to hold the automated-system line rather than override it manually.

## Can you create a dedicated affiliate program for your own course inside Skool?

Yes, and it's a separate toggle from the group-level referral commission. In the same **Affiliates** tab inside your community's admin settings, you can enable "Affiliate Settings" on an individual paid course rather than (or alongside) a percentage on general membership dues ([Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/192-how-to-set-up-member-referrals-for-your-community)). This is the standard setup for **free community + paid course** models: there's no membership fee to share a cut of, but you still want members promoting the paid product, so you set a commission specifically on course purchases. It uses the same underlying mechanics as the group-level program (unique member links, active-membership requirement, last-touch attribution) — there's no separate third-party affiliate software (Rewardful, Refersion, etc.) involved; it's entirely Skool-native and limited to a single flat percentage per course, not tiered rates or multi-product bundles.

## Is Skool's 40% recurring affiliate structure effectively a pyramid scheme?

Mechanically, no: Skool runs a **single-tier** affiliate program. It pays the person who directly referred a customer — it does not pay that referrer's referrer, and so on down a recruitment chain, which is the defining structural trait of a pyramid scheme. The product being sold (community/course software) is also real and independently useful, unlike a pyramid scheme's core requirement of no genuine product. That said, the criticism aimed at Skool isn't really about the mechanism — it's about how certain "make money on Skool" coaching communities *use* it: because a referral auto-attributes to you when a member of your group later starts their own paid community, a coach can build a group whose entire product is "how to start a Skool community," collect 40% recurring on every member who follows the exact same playbook, and let that compound indefinitely. Combined with Skool Games (a leaderboard/contest rewarding the fastest revenue growth, not member outcomes), this creates a real incentive to optimize for recruitment velocity over member results in that specific niche. The honest verdict: the platform and its affiliate program are not a pyramid scheme by definition, but "join my group to learn how to get others to start Skool groups" is the exact pattern worth being skeptical of — which is precisely why judging a community by its member-growth *quality* (organic trend, discovery-rank movement, price stability) rather than by affiliate marketing hype is the more reliable filter than the affiliate structure itself.

## Can you make money on Skool without owning a community (profiting off other people's groups)?

Yes, through two Skool-native, automated paths and two informal, negotiated ones:

- **Member affiliate commissions.** Join any Pro-plan community that has enabled affiliates, get your automatically-assigned referral link, and earn the group's set percentage (typically 10-50%) on anyone who becomes a paying member through your link — or on paid-course purchases specifically, if the owner enabled course-level affiliates instead of (or alongside) group-level ones.
- **Skool's own platform affiliate program.** Refer people to Skool itself, independent of any specific community, and earn 40% recurring on their subscription for as long as they pay — you don't need to run or even belong to a group to do this, just a Skool affiliate link.
- **Operator / revenue-share deals.** Some marketers run someone else's community day-to-day (content, engagement, onboarding) in exchange for a negotiated split of membership revenue — commonly cited arrangements are around 50/50 when an influencer supplies the audience and the operator supplies the systems. This is a private commercial agreement between two parties, not a Skool feature, so nothing about payout or terms is automated or enforced by the platform — get it in writing.
- **Paid community management roles.** Some group owners simply hire people (flat fee or hourly) to moderate, run events, or manage onboarding. Same caveat: negotiated outside Skool, no platform-level protection.

Only the first two are documented, automated, and trackable inside Skool itself. The last two require you to vet the other party directly, since there's no affiliate dashboard, contract, or dispute system covering them.
