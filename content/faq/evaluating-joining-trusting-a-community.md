## Is Skool (the platform) legit or a scam, and what's the difference between judging the platform vs. an individual community on it?

Skool the company is legit: it's a profitable, bootstrapped SaaS founded by Sam Ovens, it runs payments through standard processors, it has published [terms of service and a help center](https://help.skool.com), and it isn't going anywhere with your subscription. Its own [Better Business Bureau profile](https://www.bbb.org/us/ca/el-segundo/profile/online-shopping/skool-1216-1000037014/complaints) shows the complaints you'd expect from any billing-heavy SaaS — refund and charge disputes — not evidence the company itself is fraudulent.

But "is Skool legit" almost always gets asked about a specific paid community, not the software. That's a different question entirely. Skool is infrastructure, the same way Shopify is infrastructure: Shopify isn't a scam because some Shopify stores sell junk, and Skool isn't a scam because some Skool communities overpromise. Judge the platform on uptime, payment security, and support responsiveness. Judge the community on the creator's track record, the content behind the paywall, and whether member growth looks organic or manufactured — that's a separate evaluation, covered below.

## Is Skool a pyramid scheme or MLM — as a platform, and specifically via its 40% affiliate structure?

No. A pyramid scheme pays participants primarily for recruiting other participants rather than for a real product or service. Skool sells software — community hosting, courses, chat, gamification — and you get that software whether or not you ever refer anyone. Its [affiliate program](https://www.skool.com/affiliate-program) pays 40% of the referred customer's subscription for as long as they stay subscribed, funded out of Skool's own revenue share, not out of new members' fees flowing upward through a recruitment tree. That's a flat, single-tier referral arrangement — structurally the same as affiliate programs run by Shopify, ConvertKit, or most SaaS tools, just at a higher-than-average rate.

Where the concern is legitimate: some creators build funnels *on top of* Skool that mimic MLM mechanics — sell access to a community whose main value is teaching members to sell access to the same community to others. That's a community-level red flag (see the red-flags question below), not a platform trait. The 40% commission itself has also drawn criticism for incentivizing aggressive, hype-driven promotion of low-value communities, which is a real dynamic worth watching, but it's a marketing-quality problem, not evidence of an actual pyramid structure.

## Did Alex Hormozi mislead people about Skool, and what's the real story behind Sam Ovens and Hormozi's involvement?

Sam Ovens founded Skool around 2019, originally as the internal community backend for his own business, [Consulting.com](https://tools4skool.com/how-to/who-made-skool), before opening it to outside creators. Ovens has been CEO throughout. Alex Hormozi became a partner and equity holder starting around 2022–2023 — an investor and the marketing engine behind the brand, not a co-founder. He popularized Skool largely through the **Skool Games**, a monthly leaderboard contest with cash prizes for the highest-revenue communities, which turned a niche SaaS tool into a category-defining brand within about two years.

The "mislead" complaint people actually mean is usually about framing, not fraud: Skool Games leaderboard numbers reflect gross revenue of the platform's top handful of communities, often backed by significant paid-ad spend and existing audiences, not typical organic results for someone starting from zero. There's no evidence Hormozi lied about his role or Skool's ownership — he's been consistently described as investor/promoter, and Ovens has never stepped away from the CEO seat. The legitimate critique is that aspirational marketing (leaderboard screenshots, "anyone can do this") sets unrealistic expectations for a new community owner, which is a familiar creator-marketing problem, not a cover-up.

## What actually happens when someone spends real time and money in a paid Skool community — are the results honest?

It depends entirely on the specific community, and results are honest in proportion to how verifiable they are outside the creator's own marketing. Healthy, well-run paid communities retain **85–92% of members month over month**; if a community's churn exceeds roughly 10% a month, something in the value delivery is broken, whatever the sales page claims. On the complaint side, both [Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/www.skool.com) and BBB records show a recurring pattern: creators making guarantees on the way in, then refusing refunds or going quiet on the way out — Skool processes the payment, but the creator, not Skool, decides whether a refund is honored.

The practical way to check honesty before you pay: look for outcomes documented somewhere other than the sales page (a podcast interview, a case study on another platform, a third party mentioning the creator), and treat a community's price and membership trend as a proxy for whether it's actually delivering — communities that deliver real value tend to show slow, steady, organic growth and stable pricing, while hype-driven ones show spiky growth (one launch, then a plateau or decline) and frequent discount/price changes, which is exactly what TrustSkore's growth-trend and price-stability signals are built to surface.

## Is it worth paying to join a mastermind or high-priced community at all, and why do people charge huge sums for mastermind access?

It's worth it when the price is functioning as a genuine filter, not just a marketing lever. The core justification creators give — and it holds up — is that a real price tag raises the floor on who shows up: members with meaningful skin in the game engage more, so the peer quality inside a $200/month mastermind is usually different from a free group, and that peer access is often the actual product, not the content library. High prices also reflect direct access to a specific expert's time (live calls, hot-seats, 1:1 slots), which doesn't scale the way a static course does.

It's *not* worth it when the price is the primary marketing tool for exclusivity rather than a reflection of real access or curation — ask specifically whether the price buys live interaction with the expert or just a gated version of content that exists elsewhere for free, whether there's a defined mechanism for outcomes (not just "join and figure it out"), and whether you could articulate the specific bottleneck this is meant to unstick before you pay. If a community can't explain what changes for you in 90 days beyond "access," that price is buying exclusivity, not results.

## What red flags should you watch for before joining a paid community, and how do you verify a community is legitimate before paying?

Watch for: **no free preview content** anywhere (you can't sample the teaching style before paying anything); **artificial urgency** (countdown timers that reset, "price goes up at midnight" that never actually does); a **member count that doesn't match visible activity** (thousands of members, but the top posts of the week have single-digit replies); a **vague or conditional refund policy** ("no refunds" with no stated reason, or refunds routed entirely through the creator's discretion); **recently volatile pricing** (a jump from $27 to $97 with no added feature is a monetization move, not a value signal); and **outcome proof that only exists on the sales page itself** (screenshots the creator posted, with nothing independently verifiable).

To verify before paying: consume any public/free content the creator has produced and judge whether their actual teaching style matches how you learn; search the creator's name plus "refund" or "complaint" before buying; check how long the community has existed and whether its member trend looks like steady growth or a single promotional spike followed by a flatline; and look for the creator's presence outside Skool entirely (interviews, other platforms, third-party mentions of client results). A community with genuine value rarely needs manufactured urgency to sell itself.

## How do you choose the right community for you — what should you look at instead of just member count?

Member count is the least reliable signal available, because it's trivially inflated by free-tier vanity joins or a single paid promotion, and it says nothing about whether people are still there next month. Better signals, in order of usefulness:

- **External social proof** — does the creator have a documented track record somewhere other than their own Skool page (press, guest appearances, verifiable outside case studies)? A community built entirely on its own internal marketing is harder to verify.
- **A teaching-style preview** — actually read or watch a piece of free content before paying. Community value is delivered through how someone teaches and moderates, not just what topic they cover, and that's impossible to judge from a sales page alone.
- **Review patterns, not review counts** — look at what complaints (if any) actually cluster around. Complaints about billing friction are a different, more forgivable problem than complaints about the substance of the content being thin or recycled.
- **Growth trend and discovery rank movement** — a community that's been steadily climbing in relative rank over several months is adding and keeping members organically; one that spiked once and has been flat or sliding since was likely a launch-driven pop, not sustained demand. This is the core of what [TrustSkore](https://trustskool.com) tracks instead of a single point-in-time member count.
- **Price stability** — communities with a stable price history are generally run as a real business; communities that discount aggressively and often are usually optimizing for short-term sales pressure.

## What's the difference between free and paid community sizes, and what signals indicate members are actually staying vs. paid-and-ghosted?

Free communities grow much faster on raw numbers — removing the payment barrier typically produces **4–7× more monthly joins** than an equivalent paid community pulling from the same audience (something like 80–150 new members/month for a free group versus 15–35/month for a comparable paid one). But raw join rate says nothing about retention. Paid communities with real ongoing value retain **85–92%** of members monthly; free communities retain **68–74%** when there's an active classroom/content cadence, but as low as **41–55%** without one — the payment itself isn't what keeps people around, consistent delivered value is.

To spot "paid-and-ghosted" from the outside: compare posting velocity to member count — a 5,000-member group with three posts a week is a ghost town regardless of its headline size. Check whether recent top posts have replies from multiple distinct members, not just the admin talking to themselves. And watch whether member count keeps climbing while engagement stays flat or drops — that pattern usually means the top of the funnel (often affiliate- or ad-driven) is outrunning actual retention, exactly what a rising member count paired with a declining or flat discovery rank movement indicates.

## When should you be cautious about high-priced communities, and how do you compare similar communities in the same niche?

Be cautious when the price sits well above the median for that specific niche and tier without an obvious differentiator — live coaching time, a certification, guaranteed 1:1 access — that would justify the gap. Be extra cautious with a **brand-new community (under ~3 months old) charging a premium price with no track record**, and with any community whose price has **recently spiked** without a corresponding feature addition, since that's a value-extraction move dressed up as a quality signal.

To actually compare similar communities in a niche, put candidates side by side on: price per feature tier, growth trend over the last 3–6 months (steady positive vs. erratic), price stability history, whether any free-tier or trial content exists to sample first, and size-adjusted engagement (replies per 100 members, not raw totals — a smaller, louder community usually beats a larger, quieter one). ## Should Skool police or investigate creators running scammy groups on its platform, and is there any real way to reach Skool support?

Skool does have enforceable Terms of Service and a [reporting mechanism](https://help.skool.com/article/159-how-to-report) — you can report a specific post, comment, or member to community admins via the three-dot menu, and escalate platform-level issues (fraud, illegal content, harassment, payment disputes) to Skool directly through [help.skool.com](https://help.skool.com) or help@skool.com. Skool can and does suspend or terminate groups and creators for real Terms of Service violations.

What Skool does **not** do is proactively vet the quality or honesty of every paid community before or after launch — like most hosting platforms (Shopify, Substack, Teachable), enforcement is complaint-driven and reactive, not an editorial or content-quality gate at signup. It also explicitly does not arbitrate "is this $997 course actually worth it" disputes: refunds are decided by the individual creator, which is precisely why the BBB and Trustpilot complaint history skews toward billing and refund friction rather than platform-level fraud. If you're dealing with a specific bad-actor community, reporting to Skool directly is the real channel — expecting Skool to preemptively police every listing's marketing claims is not realistic given how the platform is built.

## My Skool community feels like a ghost town — how do you get people to actually engage?

Fix onboarding first — the first 7 days determine whether a new member becomes a regular or a ghost, and a single welcome message isn't onboarding, it's a greeting. DM new members within minutes of joining with **one specific action** (introduce yourself in the pinned thread, watch the getting-started video), not a vague "welcome, look around."

From there: replace the generic "introduce yourself below!" post with a structured, copy-paste template pinned to the top, so new members have something concrete to respond to instead of a blank prompt. Build a fixed weekly ritual — same day, same time, every week (a "Weekly Wins" or "What are you focused on this week" thread) — since consistency beats intensity; posting seven times in a day then going silent for a week kills momentum faster than posting less often but reliably. If you're using gamification levels, make sure they actually unlock something real, not just a cosmetic badge nobody notices. Ask specific, opinionated discussion questions instead of open-ended ones, and run occasional time-boxed challenges to create a shared deadline that bonds members around a common goal. Finally, audit your own posting-to-member ratio the same way an outside evaluator would (see the free-vs-paid question above) — if new members are joining faster than you're activating them, fix the activation sequence before spending more on top-of-funnel growth.
