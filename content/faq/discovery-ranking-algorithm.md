# Skool Discovery & Ranking Algorithm: Every Question Answered

Skool's Discovery page is the internal search engine that sends new members to communities, roughly 1 million visits a day, mostly through search, so where a group lands there directly affects growth. This page answers every common question about how Skool decides eligibility, rank, categories, and the Top/Trending split, using Skool's own help docs and reported cases rather than guesswork.

## When is a group eligible for Skool Discovery, and how long until it becomes visible?

A group needs to clear a minimum threshold of members, posts, and activity, plus have a complete profile (a group description, an About page with description and images, and a cover image), before Skool will list it at all, per Skool's [Discovery FAQs](https://help.skool.com/article/153-discovery-faqs). Groups that don't meet the bar show as **UNLISTED** rather than ranked, per Skool's guide on [why a group isn't visible in Discovery](https://help.skool.com/article/151-why-isnt-my-group-visible-in-discovery). Once a group qualifies, it typically becomes visible within about two hours, but Skool's team also does a manual review pass afterward, which is why a freshly-qualifying group can sit at the bottom of its category for a short window before its real position settles. In practice: finish the About page completely and get to a real, active starting roster (not a placeholder group) before expecting Discovery traffic.

## How do you rank well or higher on Discovery?

Three inputs drive rank within a category: new-member growth, activity (posts, comments, likes), and retention, per [Skool's ranking guide](https://help.skool.com/article/152-how-to-rank-higher-in-discovery). Retention carries the most weight; Skool is explicit that "the more members who keep coming back to engage, the higher your group will rank," so raw sign-up volume without people returning plateaus fast. New groups start lower by design and climb as they age and compound engagement, so a two-week-old group with strong retention can outrank a two-year-old group that's gone quiet. Two concrete levers beyond engagement itself: put your niche or core service in the group's title/name (this feeds both internal Discovery and external search), and finish the About page and cover art fully, since incomplete profiles get held back regardless of activity level. Skool also sells an optional paid **Growth Boost** that increases Discovery and off-platform ad placement on a pay-per-result basis, a separate lever from the organic factors above.

## How is the category and rank reviewed, and how can you correct your category or language?

Ranking isn't purely automated: Skool keeps a "human in the loop" that manually reviews rankings and applies the boosts and penalties covered below, specifically to catch manipulation the algorithm alone would miss, per the [Discovery FAQs](https://help.skool.com/article/153-discovery-faqs). Discovery category and language are set when a group is configured, and there is no self-serve toggle in group settings to change either if Skool assigned one wrong; you have to contact [Skool support](https://www.skool.com/contact) (help@skool.com) directly and request a manual correction, which runs through the same human-review layer and isn't instant. This is a different system from the in-group discussion "Categories" under Settings → Community → Categories, which only organizes forum posts and has no effect on your Discovery listing or rank.

## What are the ranking boosts and penalties, and does removing inactive members help your rank?

Per Skool's [Discovery FAQs](https://help.skool.com/article/153-discovery-faqs), boosts include high-quality artwork/About page, a well-defined and interesting niche, authentic human engagement, and an active owner and admin team. Penalties include bots or fake accounts, spam or low-quality engagement, a low-quality About page, taking payments off-platform, poor customer support, and an inactive owner. Removing inactive members does **not** help: Skool's own guidance says plainly that "removing inactive or level 1 members doesn't improve your rank. Some people like to lurk." The algorithm is already weighing engaged members rather than treating your total roster as one undifferentiated number, so pruning is a member-experience decision, not a ranking hack. The inverse matters more: chasing engagement through points-farming or empty like-bait prompts trips the spam/low-quality-engagement penalty and can get a group delisted from Discovery outright.

## Is Discovery ranking bugged/glitchy, and why did ranking drop after switching categories?

Some of what reads as a bug is the algorithm behaving as designed, just counterintuitively. Owners have reported sudden, large swings, one group dropped roughly 2,500 places overnight right after running a 3-day engagement challenge, per a discussion thread titled [Discovery/ranking glitches?](https://www.skool.com/community/discovery-ranking-glitches) on Skool. The likely mechanism: a short burst of low-retention activity (people showing up once for a challenge, then not returning) can register as spam-adjacent engagement rather than genuine growth, tripping the penalty side of the algorithm instead of the boost side. Category switches cause a distinct kind of drop for a separate reason: rank is computed per category, so moving categories resets your relative position, you're now compared against a new competitive set, and your accumulated growth/retention history from the old category doesn't fully carry over. Skool has also acknowledged genuine mechanical bugs (for example, an error incorrectly blocking category additions past a stated limit, reported in a thread called [Categories Bug](https://www.skool.com/community/categories-bug)), which are distinct from ranking-logic surprises. Skool's own team has effectively conceded the current system is gameable: a new ranking algorithm rolling out in May 2026 is explicitly built to "measure quality, not just activity" and be "harder to game," alongside a new Trending homepage, expanded categories, and improved search, per [coverage of Skool's 2026 Discovery update](https://skoolprep.com/skool-discovery-update-2026).

## How does Skool rank communities in its public directory (top vs. trending)?

Skool's Discovery page runs two different sorts that answer different questions. **Top** is the engagement-and-quality score described above, growth, activity, and retention within a category, weighted toward established, consistently active communities. **Trending** (the `srt=trending` view) surfaces recent growth velocity instead of accumulated standing, so a brand-new community mid hot-launch can outrank a five-year-old group on Trending while sitting far lower on Top. As part of the 2026 overhaul, Skool is adding a dedicated Trending homepage (targeted for May 2026) specifically so fast-moving communities get visibility without first needing Top-tier accumulated engagement, per [coverage of the 2026 Discovery update](https://skoolprep.com/skool-discovery-update-2026).

This split is also why a single-score directory can mislead a prospective member: a community sitting at the top of "Top" today may already be flatlining, and one climbing "Trending" could be a flash in the pan.
