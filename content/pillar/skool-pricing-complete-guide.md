# Skool Pricing: The Complete Guide (2026 Data)

Every "how much does Skool cost" article on the internet answers half the question. They'll tell you Skool the company charges $9 or $99 a month to run a community. Almost none of them tell you what members actually pay to join one, because nobody has the data. We do.

TrustSkool tracks 8,154 active Skool communities across 48 languages, scored by a growth-based TrustSkore rather than reviews. That dataset gives us something no competitor guide has: real, current pricing across 2,697 paid communities, not a handful of screenshots or a founder's guess. This guide combines that data with verified, current platform pricing to answer every real pricing question people search for about Skool, in one place.

## TL;DR: What Skool Actually Costs

**To run a community (Skool's own pricing, [verified on skool.com/pricing and help.skool.com, checked 2026-07-18](https://www.skool.com/pricing)):**

- **Hobby plan:** $9/month, 10% + $0.30 transaction fee on every payment members make
- **Pro plan:** $99/month, 2.9% + $0.30 transaction fee (3.9% + $0.30 above $899 per transaction)
- Both plans include unlimited members, courses, videos, live calls, and a custom URL
- 14-day free trial, no card required to start
- Annual billing gets you 2 months free (roughly 16.7% off)

**To join a community (our own data, 8,154 communities):**

- **59.7%** of communities are free to join (4,868 of 8,154)
- **40.3%** charge something (3,286 communities)
- Among the 2,697 communities with a real, disclosed monthly price: **median $28/month**, mean $43.78/month
- Prices range from $1/month to $999/month, with the bulk clustering between $10 and $50
- 196 communities bill yearly instead, with a median annual price of $498
- 9 listings show a suspicious flat $99,999 or $100,000/month figure. That's almost certainly Skool's placeholder for "hidden" or application-only pricing, not a real charge. We exclude these from every stat above and flag it here so you don't mistake it for a real data point.

If you've been quoted anything from $10 to $100/month by a community you're evaluating, you're within the normal range. If you're a creator wondering what to charge, $20 to $30/month is squarely where the market sits.

## How Skool Pricing Works: Hobby ($9/mo) vs. Pro ($99/mo)

Skool has run a simple two-tier model since mid-2025, replacing its original single $99/month plan. As of our check on 2026-07-18, [skool.com/pricing](https://www.skool.com/pricing) and the [Skool Payments FAQ](https://help.skool.com/article/86-subscriptions-faq) confirm the plans are:

| | Hobby | Pro |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly price | $9 | $99 |
| Transaction fee | 10% + $0.30 | 2.9% + $0.30 (3.9% + $0.30 above $899/transaction) |
| Members, courses, videos, live calls | Unlimited | Unlimited |
| Custom URL | Yes | Yes |
| Affiliate program access | Yes | Yes |

Feature-wise, Hobby and Pro are identical. There is no gated functionality, no member cap, no "upgrade to unlock courses" wall that some competing platforms use. The entire decision comes down to one variable: how much money moves through your community in membership payments, because that determines whether the $90/month price gap is cheaper or more expensive than the fee-percentage gap.

## Transaction Fees Explained: 2.9% vs. 10% (and the Catch Over $899)

This is the part most beginner guides gloss over, and it's the part that actually matters once a community starts making money.

- **Hobby plan:** flat 10% + $0.30 on every transaction, regardless of size.
- **Pro plan:** 2.9% + $0.30 on transactions up to $899, rising to 3.9% + $0.30 on any single transaction above $900.

That $899 threshold mostly matters for one-time high-ticket offers (a $997 course sold through Skool checkout, for example) rather than recurring monthly memberships, where individual charges are almost always well under $899. For a typical $28/month membership charge, Pro members pay roughly 3.7% in total fees ($1.04) versus Hobby's 11% (about $3.08), on that single transaction.

Skool takes these fees on top of standard card processing costs baked into that percentage. There's no separate Stripe bill on top for the creator. [Several third-party comparisons](https://tools4skool.com/skool/skool-transaction-fee) note this is actually cheaper than routing payments through Stripe directly once you account for international-card and subscription surcharges Stripe charges separately.

## Hobby vs. Pro: Which Plan Should You Choose? (Break-Even Point)

The math is straightforward. Pro costs $90/month more than Hobby. Pro's fee is 7.1 percentage points lower than Hobby's (10% vs. 2.9%) on transactions under $899. Solve for the revenue level where the fee savings equal $90/month:

$90 ÷ 0.071 ≈ **$1,268/month** in community payment volume.

Below roughly $1,200 to $1,300/month in revenue, Hobby is cheaper overall. Above it, Pro pays for itself in fee savings alone, and every dollar past that point is pure margin improvement.

Put this against our own distribution data: with a median paid community charging $28/month, a creator needs somewhere between 45 and 50 paying members before Pro's math beats Hobby's. Below that, most solo creators and side-project communities are better off on Hobby, upgrading only once their member count and revenue justify it. Skool lets you switch plans at any time from account settings, so there's no penalty for starting on Hobby and moving to Pro later, per [Skool's upgrade documentation](https://help.skool.com/article/193-how-to-upgrade-to-annual).

## Free Trial, Annual Billing & Growth Boost Explained

**Free trial:** Both plans include a 14-day free trial with no credit card required upfront, per [Skool's payment terms](https://help.skool.com/article/227-payment-terms-and-policy) (checked 2026-07-18). If you don't cancel before the trial ends, billing starts automatically on the plan you selected.

**Annual billing:** Toggling to yearly billing on either plan applies a "2 months free" discount, roughly 16.7% cheaper than paying monthly for 12 months. This is a discount on Skool's own subscription price; it does not change the transaction fee percentage.

**Growth Boost (30% commission):** Growth Boost is Skool's built-in paid-ads feature. It's enabled by default on new communities and uses Meta ads to drive new members to your community. When someone joins and pays through a Growth Boost-attributed ad, Skool takes a 30% commission on that specific sale, separate from and on top of the regular transaction fee. Community owners can review and turn it off in group settings before it activates; [community discussion threads on skool.com](https://www.skool.com/admins/one-thing-you-need-to-know-about-growth-boost) recommend checking this setting deliberately rather than leaving it on the default, since it's easy to miss.

## What Skool Members Actually Pay: Our Data

This is the number nobody else can give you with real evidence: across our tracked dataset of 8,154 communities, **2,697 have a real, disclosed monthly price** (we exclude 9 listings priced at exactly $99,999 or $100,000/month, discussed below).

- **Median: $28/month**
- **Mean: $43.78/month** (pulled upward by a tail of premium high-ticket communities)
- **Minimum: $1/month**
- **Maximum: $999/month** (excluding the sentinel values)
- **196 communities** bill annually instead of monthly, with a **median annual price of $498** (equivalent to about $41.50/month, somewhat above the monthly median, consistent with annual plans skewing toward more established, higher-value communities)

The gap between the $28 median and $43.78 mean tells its own story: most paid Skool communities charge modestly, but a meaningful minority charge a lot, and that minority pulls the average up. If you only remember one number from this guide, remember the median, not the mean. It's the more honest description of what a typical paying member actually experiences.

### A note on the $99,999 placeholder

Nine listings in our dataset show a price of exactly $99,999 or $100,000 per month. No Skool community charges six figures monthly; this is almost certainly a sentinel or placeholder value Skool's platform inserts for pricing that's hidden, application-only, or not yet configured, rather than a genuine charge. We exclude these 9 from every statistic in this guide. We're disclosing it here because we'd rather flag a data quirk than let you stumble on it and wonder if our numbers are broken. They're not; we just didn't want to pretend $99,999/month is a real price point.

If you're evaluating a specific community's price, use this table as a sanity check: under $10 is budget/hobby territory, $10-$50 is the mainstream range covering roughly 76% of all paid communities, and anything above $50 should come with a correspondingly higher level of access, coaching, or done-for-you support to justify the premium bracket.

## How Pricing Varies by Niche

Our own dataset gives a strong picture of the overall price distribution across all 8,154 communities, but we don't yet have a clean niche-by-niche cross-tabulation to publish with the same confidence as the headline numbers above, so we won't fabricate one. What we can responsibly report comes from third-party niche research, cited as such:

- Business, AI, and "make money online" communities tend to skew toward the higher end of the distribution, often landing in the $30-$99+ range, reflecting both higher perceived ROI and audiences more accustomed to paying for information products, per [Codingoblin's niche profitability analysis](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches).
- Fitness, hobby, and personal-development communities more often sit in the $10-$30 range, closer to a "coffee subscription" price point than a "business tool" price point.
- An independent analysis of the [top 1,000 Skool communities by Joe Apfelbaum](https://joeapfelbaum.substack.com/p/skool-communities-analysis-top-1000) found a different free/paid split (roughly 60% paid) than Codingoblin's separate sample of ~2,629 communities (roughly 30% paid). We flag this disagreement rather than pick a side: sample selection (top 1,000 by some ranking vs. a broader crawl) plausibly explains most of the gap, and our own figure across all 8,154 communities, 40.3% paid, sits between the two. If you're citing a "percent of Skool communities are paid" statistic, know that published third-party numbers vary by roughly 2x depending on which slice of Skool they sampled.

## Revenue Benchmarks: Inside $5K, $10K and $50K/Month Communities

Independent revenue case studies (not our own dataset, since TrustSkool tracks pricing and growth signals, not creator revenue disclosures) give a rough sense of what different revenue tiers look like in practice, per [Communipass's Skool revenue benchmarks](https://communipass.com/blog/skool-revenue-benchmarks-2026):

- **~$5K/month** communities typically run 150-250 paying members at a mid-range price point (roughly our $10-$30 band), often a single creator handling content and light community management.
- **~$10K/month** communities usually combine a larger paid base (300-500 members) with either a slightly higher price point or a mix of monthly and annual billers, plus at least one team member handling moderation or support.
- **~$50K/month** communities are the exception rather than the norm, and tend to either carry premium pricing (our $100+ band) with a smaller, higher-touch member base, or blend a large low-cost community with upsells into higher-ticket coaching or done-for-you services outside the core Skool subscription.

Take these as directional, not gospel. Revenue self-reports are notoriously optimistic and rarely audited. Our own dataset is a stronger source for "what does this community charge" than for "how much does this community make."

## Skool's Affiliate Program: The 40% Recurring Commission Explained

Separate from what communities charge their own members, Skool runs its own affiliate program for people who refer new community owners to the platform: **40% recurring commission for the life of the referred customer**, with a 60-day cookie window and a $100 minimum payout, per [Skool's affiliate program page](https://www.skool.com/affiliate-program). On the $99/month Pro plan, that's $39.60/month, indefinitely, for every referred customer who stays subscribed. This is a real driver of why so much "Skool pricing" content exists online: a large share of it is written by affiliates with a financial incentive to make the platform sound as cheap and simple as possible. TrustSkool has no such incentive on the platform-fee side; our monetization is ref-coded outbound links to individual communities, not to Skool itself.

## Billing, Cancellation & Refund Policy: What to Expect

- **Refunds:** Per [Skool's payment terms](https://help.skool.com/article/227-payment-terms-and-policy) and [payments FAQ](https://help.skool.com/article/86-subscriptions-faq), subscription fees are non-refundable once paid, and accrue from the start of each billing term regardless of usage. Community owners can offer their own members a refund at their discretion for a member's most recent transaction, but Skool itself does not guarantee refunds on a creator's behalf.
- **Cancellation:** Canceling stops future renewals; it does not retroactively refund the current term. For creators canceling their own owner subscription, their group and its content are affected per Skool's standard subscription-lapse handling; check your specific plan's terms before canceling if you have an active paid membership base, since member access continuity depends on your plan status.
- **Auto-renewal:** Free trials convert automatically into paid subscriptions unless canceled before the trial ends, per the same payment terms documentation.

## Common Pricing Complaints (and How to Avoid Them)

The most frequent member and creator complaints we see referenced across help forums and review sites aren't about the headline $9/$99 numbers; they're about surprises:

1. **Growth Boost's 30% commission catching creators off guard**, because it's on by default. Check your group settings the day you launch a paid community, not after your first sale.
2. **The transaction-fee gap being invisible until you do the math**, since Hobby's $9 looks like the "obviously cheaper" choice until a growing community crosses the roughly $1,268/month break-even point and starts overpaying in fees relative to Pro.
3. **Members not realizing subscriptions auto-renew after a free trial**, leading to surprise charges. This is a platform-wide subscription-commerce pattern, not unique to Skool, but worth stating plainly since it's a frequent search query.
4. **Difficulty finding the cancel button**, a recurring theme in independent reviews; cancellation lives in account/community settings rather than being surfaced prominently in the main navigation.

## Skool Pricing vs. Circle, Mighty Networks, Kajabi, Whop & Teachable

Skool's flat, low-cost, transaction-fee-funded model is a genuinely different pricing philosophy than most competitors, which lean on higher monthly platform fees and lower or no transaction cuts:

- **Circle** and **Mighty Networks** typically start well above Skool's $9-$99 range on entry-level paid tiers and scale pricing by member count or feature tier rather than by a simple two-plan structure, per comparisons like [Mighty Networks' own Skool vs. Circle breakdown](https://www.mightynetworks.com/resources/skool-vs-circle) and [Communipass's four-way platform comparison](https://communipass.com/blog/kajabi-vs-skool-vs-circle-vs-mighty-networks-which-gets-more-people-to-actually-complete).
- **Kajabi** bundles course hosting, email marketing, and a community feature into higher-priced all-in-one plans, generally a better fit for creators who want marketing automation bundled in rather than a standalone community product.
- **Whop** and **Teachable** each have their own fee structures (Whop skews toward a marketplace/discovery model with its own take rate; Teachable is closer to a traditional course-hosting platform with tiered plans and per-transaction fees on lower tiers).

The practical takeaway: Skool is close to the cheapest entry point in the category at $9/month, and its transaction-fee model means cost scales with revenue rather than requiring an upfront commitment to a pricier plan before you've proven the community can generate income.

## Is Skool Worth the Price? Verdict by Use Case

**Running a free community:** Hobby's $9/month is close to a rounding error, and since there are no payments, the transaction fee never applies. This is close to the cheapest "real" community platform option available.

**Running a paid community, early stage:** Start on Hobby. At a typical $28/month price point (our dataset median) and a small member base, the 10% fee costs less in absolute dollars than the $90/month gap to Pro would save you in fee percentage.

**Running a paid community, once you clear ~$1,200-$1,300/month in payment volume:** Switch to Pro. The fee savings alone justify the higher monthly cost, and it compounds as you grow.

**Running a course-creator business needing built-in marketing automation:** Skool is deliberately minimal here. If you need email sequences, funnels, or advanced marketing tooling bundled with your community, weigh Kajabi or a Skool-plus-separate-email-tool stack against Skool's simplicity.

## Skool Pricing FAQ

### Is it free to create or join a Skool community?

Yes, creating a Skool account and starting a community is free; you only pay if you choose a paid plan (Hobby or Pro) to run your own community, or if you join someone else's community that charges membership fees. In our dataset, 59.7% of all 8,154 tracked communities are free to join.

### Does Skool take a cut of community payments?

Yes. Every payment a member makes through Skool's checkout is subject to a transaction fee set by the group owner's plan: 10% + $0.30 on Hobby, or 2.9%-3.9% + $0.30 on Pro depending on transaction size, per [Skool's payments FAQ](https://help.skool.com/article/86-subscriptions-faq).

### What are Skool's transaction fees on the Hobby vs. Pro plan?

Hobby charges a flat 10% + $0.30 per transaction. Pro charges 2.9% + $0.30 on transactions up to $899, rising to 3.9% + $0.30 on any single transaction of $900 or more. Both figures were verified directly on [help.skool.com](https://help.skool.com/article/86-subscriptions-faq) on 2026-07-18.

### Can I switch between the Hobby and Pro plans?

Yes, you can upgrade or downgrade at any time from your community's billing settings, per [Skool's upgrade documentation](https://help.skool.com/article/193-how-to-upgrade-to-annual). There's no lock-in period tying you to your initial choice.

### What is Skool's refund policy?

Subscription fees are non-refundable once charged, per [Skool's payment terms](https://help.skool.com/article/227-payment-terms-and-policy). For member-level purchases within a community, the group owner has discretion to issue refunds on the member's most recent transaction, but Skool itself does not guarantee or process refunds on a creator's behalf.

### How long is Skool's free trial?

Both Hobby and Pro include a 14-day free trial with no credit card required to start. If you don't cancel before day 14, billing begins automatically on the plan you selected.

### How much does the average Skool community cost to join, and what do paid communities typically charge per month?

Across 2,697 paid communities in our own 8,154-community dataset, the median price is $28/month and the mean is $43.78/month. Prices span from $1/month to $999/month (excluding a small number of placeholder listings), with the largest single cluster, about 35% of paid communities, charging between $10 and $29/month.

### What are the most profitable Skool community niches?

Third-party research suggests business, AI, and "make money online" niches skew toward higher price points (often $30-$99+), while fitness, hobby, and personal-development communities more often land in the $10-$30 range, per [Codingoblin's niche analysis](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches). We don't yet have a niche-level breakdown in our own dataset to confirm this at the same confidence level as our overall pricing stats.

### Is Skool worth $99 a month?

It depends entirely on your transaction volume. Pro's $99/month only pays for itself once your community processes roughly $1,268/month or more in member payments, because that's the point where Pro's lower fee percentage saves more than $90/month compared to staying on Hobby. Below that revenue level, Hobby is the cheaper option overall.

### How does Skool's pricing compare to Circle, Mighty Networks, and Kajabi?

Skool's $9-$99/month range with a percentage-based transaction fee is generally cheaper at entry than Circle, Mighty Networks, or Kajabi, which typically charge higher flat monthly fees, especially on plans with more members or features, per [comparisons from Mighty Networks](https://www.mightynetworks.com/resources/skool-vs-circle) and [Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/kajabi-vs-skool-vs-circle-vs-mighty-networks-which-gets-more-people-to-actually-complete). Skool's tradeoff is fewer built-in marketing/automation features than an all-in-one platform like Kajabi.

### What happens to my group if I cancel my Skool subscription?

Canceling stops future billing renewals but does not refund the current paid term; per Skool's [payment terms](https://help.skool.com/article/227-payment-terms-and-policy), your subscription remains active through the period already paid for. If you're a creator with paying members, review your plan's specific handling of member access before canceling, since continuity depends on your account's subscription status.

### What is Skool's Growth Boost, and does it cost extra?

Growth Boost is Skool's built-in Meta-ads feature that promotes your community to new potential members, turned on by default in group settings. It doesn't cost anything upfront, but Skool takes a 30% commission on any sale that results from a Growth Boost-driven referral, separate from your normal transaction fee. Community owners can review and disable it before it activates.

### When did Skool add the cheaper $9/month Hobby plan?

Skool introduced the lower-cost Hobby tier in 2025 (community posts referencing the change, such as ["New $9 Skool plan is here"](https://www.skool.com/educate/new-9-skool-plan-is-here), date to around July 2025), moving from a single $99/month plan to the current two-tier Hobby/Pro structure. This guide reflects the current structure as verified live on 2026-07-18.

### Is there an annual billing discount on Skool plans?

Yes. Toggling annual billing on either Hobby or Pro applies a "2 months free" discount, equivalent to roughly 16.7% off the monthly-billed total, per [skool.com/pricing](https://www.skool.com/pricing).

### What's the break-even point between Hobby and Pro?

Roughly $1,268/month in community payment volume, calculated from the $90/month price gap divided by the 7.1 percentage-point difference in transaction fees (10% vs. 2.9%). Below that revenue level, Hobby costs less overall; above it, Pro's lower fee percentage saves more than its higher monthly price.

### Does Skool support PayPal, and are there currency or payout fees?

Skool's checkout is card-based through its payment processing partner rather than PayPal; specific payout schedules and any currency-conversion handling for non-USD payouts are detailed in [Skool's payments FAQ](https://help.skool.com/article/86-subscriptions-faq), which we'd recommend checking directly if cross-currency payouts affect your specific situation, since exact conversion mechanics weren't fully specified in the public documentation we reviewed.

### What percentage of Skool communities are actually paid vs. free?

In our own dataset of 8,154 communities, 40.3% charge for membership and 59.7% are free. Two independent third-party studies disagree with each other on this figure: one analysis of roughly 2,629 communities found about 30% paid, while a separate analysis of the top 1,000 communities found about 60% paid. Our 40.3% sits between the two, likely because we track a broader and larger cross-section (8,154 communities across 48 languages) rather than a curated top-N list or a smaller sample.
