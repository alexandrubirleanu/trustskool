## Frequently Asked Questions

### What counts as a "Hobbies" community on Skool?
Skool's Hobbies category includes crafting and making (woodworking, sewing, 3D printing), creative skills (photography, painting, drawing), gaming and tabletop groups, cooking and baking clubs, and collector or enthusiast communities for things like sneakers, cars, or vinyl records. The common thread is a shared, non-professional interest rather than a business or career outcome.

### Are free hobby communities on Skool worth joining?
Often yes. Because most hobby communities aren't selling a transformation or income outcome, many good ones are free or very low cost, funded by an owner who wants a home base for their audience rather than a paid product. A free community with strong, steady member growth is frequently a better sign than a paid one with flat or declining numbers.

### How do you tell a real hobby community from a disguised sales funnel?
Look at what the price is actually for. If a "hobby" community's core pitch is a course, coaching program, or product upsell rather than ongoing peer interaction around the hobby itself, it's closer to a funnel than a community. Erratic price changes, tracked in TrustSkore's price stability signal, are also a common tell that a group is being monetized aggressively rather than maintained long-term.

### Do hobby communities on Skool need to be huge to be good?
No. Hobby communities tend to reward depth over size. A tight 200-person group of serious film photographers can be more useful than a 5,000-member general "creativity" community. That's why TrustSkore weighs growth trajectory and engagement momentum rather than raw member count alone.
