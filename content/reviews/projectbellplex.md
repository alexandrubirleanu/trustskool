# Project Bellplex Review 2026: Is $45/Month Worth It for Kettlebell Training?

Project Bellplex is a $45-a-month Skool community with 1,205 members built entirely around one piece of equipment: the kettlebell. Its description makes the pitch for accessibility explicit: "Build functional strength, conditioning and mobility using kettlebells from home! You don't need a ton of space, equipment or time!"

## What's Actually Inside

The three named outcomes, functional strength, conditioning, and mobility, cover more ground than a typical "just lift heavy" program, kettlebells are often used specifically because they combine strength work with cardiovascular conditioning and joint mobility in a single tool. The repeated emphasis on needing minimal space, equipment, and time positions this directly at people training at home, likely without a full gym setup, rather than gym-goers looking to add kettlebells to an already extensive routine.

## Pros

- Single-equipment focus (kettlebells) keeps the barrier to entry low: no need for a home gym full of machines.
- Explicit targeting of time- and space-constrained members, addressing a real obstacle that keeps many people from training consistently.
- Combines strength, conditioning, and mobility goals rather than treating them as separate programs.

## Cons

The description doesn't specify how many kettlebells or what weight range is needed, nor the experience level assumed, so newcomers to kettlebell training specifically may want to confirm beginner-friendliness before joining. We also found no independent reviews of this community beyond its own Skool listing.

## Who It's a Good Fit For

People training at home with limited space and equipment who want a single, well-rounded training tool covering strength, conditioning, and mobility rather than juggling multiple programs and pieces of equipment.

## Who Should Skip It

Anyone who already trains at a fully equipped gym and isn't specifically interested in kettlebell-based training, or who prefers barbell- or machine-based programming, likely won't get much use from a kettlebell-only community. People chasing maximal strength numbers specifically may also find a mobility-and-conditioning-inclusive program less specialized than a pure powerlifting approach.

At $45 a month for a single-equipment program, it's worth weighing that price against how consistently new programming actually gets delivered, which is easier to judge by looking at the community's real activity and growth than at the pitch alone.
