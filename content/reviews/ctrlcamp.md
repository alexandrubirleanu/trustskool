# CTRL Camp Review 2026: Is $9/Month Worth It for Music Licensing to TV & Ads?

CTRL Camp, full name "CTRL Camp | Music for TV & Ads," is a $9-a-month Skool community with 341 members. The pitch is aimed at musicians and producers who want their tracks earning money through sync licensing: "Your music should be earning you money. TV & ads will pay. We'll show you what to make and connect you to licensing companies."

## What's Actually Inside

Two things stand out in the description. First, it promises guidance on what to actually make, meaning the kind of music that gets placed in TV shows and ad campaigns, rather than just general music production advice. Second, it promises connections to licensing companies, suggesting the community is meant to bridge the gap between having finished tracks and getting them in front of buyers, not just teaching the craft in isolation.

## Pros

- Addresses both sides of sync licensing, what to create and who to pitch it to, rather than only one half of the problem.
- A specific, income-oriented framing for musicians, positioning music as a monetizable product rather than only a creative pursuit.
- At $9 a month, the price is low relative to the potential value of even one successful sync placement.

## Cons

- The description doesn't name which licensing companies members get connected to, or detail the process for submitting music, so the concrete mechanics of the "connection" promise aren't spelled out.
- No independent reviews of this community were found beyond its own Skool listing, so there's no outside verification of actual placement success rates among members.

## Who It's a Good Fit For

Musicians and producers who already have a body of work and want to learn how to make it commercially viable for TV and advertising placements, and who want a lower-cost way to start exploring that path.

## Who Should Skip It

Musicians focused purely on artistic release or live performance rather than commercial licensing are likely to find the sync-focused framing beside the point. Anyone expecting a guaranteed placement or income figure, rather than guidance and connections, should also treat those as separate promises the description doesn't explicitly make.
