# PCB Design Skool Review 2026: Is It Worth $9 a Month?

PCB Design Skool is a 352-member community priced at $9 a month, aimed at hardware engineers who want to learn how to design printed circuit boards professionally. Its description names specific technical stages, "stackup to layout to signal integrity & EMC", which signals a genuinely technical, engineering-focused offer rather than a general electronics hobby page.

## What's Actually Inside

The description lists three named competencies: board stackup design, layout, and signal integrity plus EMC (electromagnetic compatibility) considerations. That's a meaningful span, stackup and layout are foundational PCB design steps, while signal integrity and EMC are the kind of advanced topics that trip up engineers moving from simple boards to more demanding, high-speed designs. The description also mentions courses alongside a "global community of hardware engineers," suggesting the offer combines structured lessons with peer discussion rather than being purely one or the other.

The "global" framing of the community is worth noting for a technical niche like PCB design, where standards, component availability, and manufacturing partners can vary meaningfully by region, so it's reasonable to expect a broad, general-principles approach rather than guidance tied to one country's supply chain.

## Pros

- Names specific, credible technical topics (stackup, layout, signal integrity, EMC) rather than vague electronics buzzwords, which is a good sign for an engineering-focused community.
- A low monthly price ($9) relative to the technical depth implied by the topics listed.
- Combines structured courses with a stated community of hardware engineers, useful for getting feedback on real design questions.

## Cons

The description doesn't specify which PCB design software or tools the courses are built around, worth confirming if you use a specific CAD package. We also found no independent reviews of this community beyond its own Skool listing.

## Who It's a Good Fit For

Hardware engineers and electronics hobbyists moving from basic PCB layout into more advanced concerns like signal integrity and EMC compliance, who want both course material and a community of peers.

## Who Should Skip It

Complete beginners with no electronics background may find the named topics, especially signal integrity and EMC, too advanced without foundational knowledge first, and hobbyists only interested in simple, single-layer boards may not need the more advanced material either.
