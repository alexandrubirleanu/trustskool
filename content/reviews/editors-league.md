# Editors League Review 2026: Worth Joining to Land Editing Clients?

Plenty of editing communities teach software and technique; fewer explicitly pair that with the business side of landing paying work. Editors League tries to do both, describing itself as "the complete blueprint" covering "every aspect of editing, from editing excellence to landing premium clients." It's a free Skool community with 1,429 members, in English, at a size that suggests it's become a fairly established gathering point within the video editing corner of Skool specifically.

## What's Actually Inside

The description points to two connected tracks: craft (getting genuinely good at editing) and client acquisition (specifically "premium" clients, implying higher-paying work rather than volume, low-rate gigs). Framing it as "the complete blueprint" suggests the community positions itself as covering the full path from skill-building through to actually getting hired, rather than one half of that equation. A lot of editors are genuinely skilled with the software but struggle specifically with pricing, pitching, and finding clients willing to pay premium rates, so pairing the two explicitly addresses a real, common gap.

## Pros

- Free to join, so the combined craft-plus-business pitch can be tested without financial risk.
- Explicit focus on premium clients rather than just any client, which signals an emphasis on rate and positioning, not just volume of work.
- Covers both the creative and business sides of editing in one place, useful for editors who are skilled but struggle with the client-finding side, or vice versa.

## Cons

The description doesn't specify which editing software, niches (say, YouTube, short-form, corporate, or film), or client-finding channels are taught, so it's worth checking whether the specific focus matches your editing specialty. We found no independent reviews of this community beyond its own listing.

## Who It's a Good Fit For

Video editors who already have some skill but want structured guidance on turning that into premium-paying client work, rather than editors purely looking for technical tutorials with no business component.

## Who Should Skip It

Complete beginners with zero editing experience may want a more fundamentals-focused resource first, and editors who already have a full client roster and just want advanced technical training may find the client-acquisition half less relevant to them. It's worth checking which side of that blueprint gets more actual attention week to week before assuming both halves receive equal weight.
