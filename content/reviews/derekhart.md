# Marriage Repair Review 2026: Is It Worth $77/Month?

Marriage Repair is a $77/month Skool community with just under 2,000 members, built around a distinctive live-format premise: members watch real couples work through relationship issues in live sessions each week, rather than working exclusively from pre-recorded modules or one-on-one coaching alone.

## What's Actually Inside

Based on the community's own description, the core offering is weekly live sessions where real couples are guided through repair conversations in front of the group, with the stated idea being that the surface-level argument usually isn't the real issue. The group is designed to help members find the conversation underneath the conversation that's actually driving conflict. Notably, the description states you don't need your spouse present to begin, suggesting the program is built to accommodate one partner working on the relationship even if the other isn't yet participating.

## Pros

- A distinctive live-demonstration format, watching real couples work through issues, rather than only generic advice content, which could make abstract relationship concepts more concrete for members watching.
- Explicitly designed to work even if only one spouse joins, lowering the barrier for people whose partner isn't ready to participate yet.
- Nearly 2,000 paying members at $77/month is a meaningful base for a niche relationship-coaching community, suggesting real ongoing demand.

## Cons

- At $77/month, this sits well above the price of most Skool communities, so it's worth confirming session frequency and format fit your schedule and needs before committing to an ongoing subscription.
- We found no independent reviews of this specific community beyond its own platform listing, so there's no outside track record to weigh against the price.

## Who It's a Good Fit For

One or both partners in a struggling marriage who want to see real repair conversations modeled live, and who are comfortable with a group-watching format rather than fully private coaching. It may also suit someone whose spouse isn't yet willing to seek help, since the program's own framing is explicitly built to let one partner start the work solo.

## Who Should Skip It

Couples who need or prefer strictly private, one-on-one therapy, or anyone in a situation involving abuse or safety concerns, which is better addressed directly by a licensed therapist rather than a group program.
