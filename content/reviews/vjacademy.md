# VJ Academy Review 2026: Free Resource Hub for Visual Jockeys

VJ Academy calls itself "the ultimate resource hub for VJs of all skill levels," a free, 651-member Skool community built around visual jockeying, the art of live video mixing and projection typically used at concerts, clubs, and events. The description lists over 100 tutorial classrooms, VJ loops, a global community, and plugins as the core assets.

## What's Actually Inside

The four elements named in the description map fairly directly to what a working VJ actually needs: tutorials (the "100+ Tutorial Classroom" for learning technique and software), VJ loops (pre-made visual content to mix and perform with live), plugins (tools that extend whatever VJ software members are using), and a global community for sharing work, troubleshooting technical setups, and networking with other visual performers. That combination of raw content assets plus instructional material suggests VJ Academy functions as both a learning resource and an asset library, rather than just one or the other.

## Pros

- Free to join, which is notable given that VJ loop packs and plugin bundles are often sold individually elsewhere.
- Explicitly serves "all skill levels," so it isn't gatekept for experienced VJs only.
- Combines instructional tutorials with usable assets (loops, plugins), giving members something to apply immediately rather than just theory.

## Cons

- No independent reviews found beyond the platform listing, so the quality and currency of the 100+ tutorials and loop library can't be verified in advance.
- VJ software and hardware setups vary widely (Resolume, TouchDesigner, MadMapper, and others), and the description doesn't specify which tools the tutorials and plugins are built around.

## Who It's a Good Fit For

Aspiring or working visual jockeys looking for a free hub of tutorials, loops, and plugins, along with a community of other VJs to learn from and network with.

## Who Should Skip It

Video editors or motion designers looking for general video editing skills rather than live VJ performance techniques, since the two disciplines overlap but aren't identical.
