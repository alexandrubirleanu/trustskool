# The Most Common Skool Community Niches in 2026 (and Which Ones Are Growing)

Skool has grown into a genuinely large ecosystem, over 170,000 communities and more than 25 million users as of 2026 ([Thoughtlytics](https://www.thoughtlytics.com/newsletter/skool-growth-strategies)), but the niches inside that ecosystem are not evenly distributed. A few categories dominate, a few are quietly profitable without being huge, and a few get talked about more than the data actually supports. Here's the landscape as it stands.

## The Biggest Categories by Count

An analysis of 1,000 Skool communities sorted into 16 distinct categories found Education (152 communities), AI/Automation (94), and Finance (50) as the three largest defined themes ([Joe Apfelbaum](https://joeapfelbaum.substack.com/p/skool-communities-analysis-top-1000)). A separate, larger sample of 2,629 groups found 341 to be AI-related, roughly 13% of the entire sample ([Codingoblin](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches)).

## AI and Automation: Still the Center of Gravity

AI and automation isn't just the largest category by count, it also produces the platform's single biggest communities. AI Automation Society is the largest free community on Skool, with around 425,000 members, ahead of Skoolers, the official Skool platform community, at roughly 165,000 ([Codingoblin](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches)). Three of Skool's five largest groups overall are AI-related, and the pattern across the category is consistent: the biggest AI communities are free and function as a funnel into a paid tier, such as AI Automation Society Plus, which charges $99/month and has grown to 3,666 members ([Codingoblin](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches)). Even a small conversion rate off a free community that size adds up to substantial recurring revenue.

## Finance and Trading

Finance and investment communities show a notably different economics profile: roughly a 48% paid ratio with a $69 median price ([Codingoblin](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches)). The logic is straightforward, members are paying for signals, analysis, and education that ties directly to income, which supports a much higher willingness to pay than more general educational content.

## Real Estate

Real estate investing communities have become one of the more serious, consistently active verticals on the platform. Investors tend to be a strong fit for the format: financially motivated, already spending on education, and benefiting directly from peer networking with others doing live deals ([Discount Property Investor](https://www.discountpropertyinvestor.com/blog/best-free-real-estate-investing-community-on-skool-in-2026)).

## Health, Fitness, and the Peptides Niche

This category is a useful lesson in how "niche size" and "niche monetizability" diverge. General fitness content performs poorly as a paid category, only about 17% of general fitness communities are paid, because so much of that content is freely available elsewhere. Narrower health niches perform very differently: nutrition-specific and peptide/biohacking-focused communities monetize meaningfully better than broad fitness content, precisely because they serve a more specific, harder-to-Google need ([Codingoblin](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches)).

## Faceless Content Creation

Communities teaching "faceless" content strategies, building YouTube channels, short-form content, or automated content pipelines without appearing on camera, have grown as a distinct niche, ranging from low-cost entry communities (some as low as $5/month) up to more structured paid frameworks combining strategy courses with AI production tooling ([Unil](https://unil.ink/blog/best-skool-communities-2026)).

## Developer, Affiliate, and Crypto: The Highest Median Prices

Across the niche landscape, developer-focused, affiliate marketing, real estate, and crypto communities command the highest median prices on the platform, often in the $97-$150/month range, because members believe the knowledge will directly generate a return that exceeds the subscription cost ([Codingoblin](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches)).

## An Honest Gap: Credit Repair and Similar "Money" Niches

Credit repair and adjacent money-focused niches come up frequently in anecdotal discussion of Skool as a platform for information products, but the published community analyses referenced above don't break out credit repair as its own tracked category with hard numbers. Treat any specific growth or pricing claim about credit repair communities on Skool as unverified until a dataset actually isolates that niche; it likely sits inside the broader finance/money-making bucket rather than as a distinctly measured segment.

## What This Means If You're Choosing a Niche

The pattern across the categories that actually monetize well, finance, real estate, developer, crypto, peptides/biohacking, is specificity: a narrow, well-defined problem that a broad free alternative doesn't already solve well. The advice that shows up consistently across niche breakdowns is to dominate a narrow niche first and expand only once value is proven, rather than launching broad and hoping volume compensates ([Creatorflow](https://creatorflow.so/blog/best-creator-communities-skool-whop/)).

Whichever niche you're drawn to, the category itself is a weaker signal of quality than the actual growth and engagement happening inside a specific community.
