# Best Skool Communities for SaaS and Software Builders in 2026

Building and monetizing software has become a specific enough use case on Skool that it stands apart from the broader "AI automation" category it often gets lumped into. The communities here are less about general AI education and more about a concrete outcome: shipping a SaaS product, a white-label AI tool, or a Go-High-Level agency that can be sold as a service. Our dataset tracks 14 communities built specifically around this goal, ranging from a free workshop with over 5,000 members to a $197/month mastermind-style program. Every one of them lists English as its primary language, so if you're comparing options from outside an English-speaking market, that's one filter you won't need to apply. Here's how the real numbers stack up.

## How to Pick Between Them

Worth noting first: this list splits almost exactly down the middle, five communities charge nothing and five charge between $7 and $197/month, and the free side includes both the largest community here (5,097 members) and some of the smallest. Price and size don't move together in any clean way in this niche, which is itself a useful data point. It means you can't shortcut the decision by assuming "bigger must mean free" or "paid must mean better," you actually have to look at each one.

None of these numbers should be the only thing you look at. A free community with 5,000 members and a $19/month community with 300 members can both be the right call, depending on what you actually need. Larger free communities tend to work well if you want volume, more people building in public, more examples to learn from, more chances to run into a collaborator. Smaller paid communities tend to work better when you want direct access to the founder or a tighter curriculum, since a $197 or $19 monthly price usually buys accountability and structure a free group of thousands can't offer at that scale.

Price alone doesn't tell you whether a community delivers. A $7/month whitelabel community and a $197/month mastermind are solving different problems for different people, and member count is a weak proxy for whether a group is still active rather than one that grew fast once and went quiet. Before joining anything on this list, check its TrustSkore on TrustSkool, growth trend, posting activity, and how members actually describe the experience inside, not just the headline number on the join page.
