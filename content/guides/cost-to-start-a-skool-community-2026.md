# Starting a Skool Community in 2026: What It Actually Costs and Takes

The platform fee to start a Skool community is small. The realistic cost of building one that actually works is almost entirely time, and most of the failures come from underestimating that second part. Here's a grounded breakdown of both.

## The Direct Platform Cost

Skool offers two plans. The Hobby plan is $9/month with a 10% transaction fee on payments processed through the platform. The Pro plan is $99/month with a 2.9% + $0.30 transaction fee, and no additional platform cut on individual sales up to $899, above that threshold, Pro sales are charged a tiered 3.9% + $0.30 ([Kourses](https://kourses.com/skool-pricing/); [Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/86-subscriptions-faq)). Skool also offers a 14-day free trial for new community owners setting up their own subscription ([Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/86-subscriptions-faq)).

The practical rule: the $9 Hobby plan is cheaper for as long as your community's monthly revenue stays under roughly $1,300; once you cross that, the 10% transaction fee costs more than the $90/month gap to Pro ([Kourses](https://kourses.com/skool-pricing/)). Either way, the platform itself is not the expensive part of starting a community.

## The Real Cost Is Time, Not Money

A realistic first 90 days looks less like a launch and more like a daily content operation. One breakdown of the process recommends: post one short video daily, write three long-form posts per week inside the community, run a weekly free workshop, and personally DM the most active 10% of free members with an invitation to the paid tier ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/how-to-monetize-a-skool-community-30-day-roadmap-2026/)). None of that requires a budget. All of it requires consistent daily effort for months before it compounds.

## The 90-Day Retention Math You Can't Skip

Retention, not signups, is where most early revenue quietly disappears. The average churn rate across Skool communities sits around 18%, with under 10% considered good. Churn is highest in the first three months, roughly 20%, before dropping to around 10% by day 90 and further to about 2% after six months ([Skootcrm](https://www.skootcrm.com/blog/how-to-reduce-skool-churn); [Educate](https://www.skool.com/educate/average-churn-levels)). Critically, roughly 60% of the members who eventually churn make that decision in their very first week ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/how-to-monetize-a-skool-community-30-day-roadmap-2026/)). That means the highest-leverage "cost" in your first 90 days isn't acquisition, it's a functioning onboarding sequence that catches people before that first-week decision is made.

## The Most Common Early Mistakes

**Obsessing over acquisition, ignoring retention.** Most new operators pour their effort into free trials and top-of-funnel content while roughly 60-70% of their eventually-lost revenue is hiding in avoidable early churn, not a lack of new signups ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/how-to-monetize-a-skool-community-30-day-roadmap-2026/)).

**Treating it like a product launch instead of a trust cycle.** Skool community monetization tends to work on a trust-and-result cycle, members need to see real value accumulate over weeks, rather than a single launch-week spike that fades ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/how-to-monetize-a-skool-community-30-day-roadmap-2026/)).

**No onboarding DM sequence.** A simple three-touch sequence, welcome, progress check-in, problem-focused nudge, directly targets the week-one churn window described above and is one of the highest-leverage, zero-cost things a new operator can build before worrying about anything else ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/how-to-monetize-a-skool-community-30-day-roadmap-2026/)).

**Skipping gamification.** Communities that implement structured gamification, points, levels, leaderboards, see roughly 24% better member retention per quarter, with leaderboard-driven communities showing 70-80% retention versus 50-60% for communities without it ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/skool-revenue-benchmarks-2026/)). This is a native Skool feature, not a paid add-on, so skipping it is a pure opportunity cost.

## A Realistic Picture of Month One

Budget for the platform fee itself ($9 or $99, depending on your plan choice) and treat everything else as time: daily content, a real onboarding sequence, and enough consistency to survive the highest-churn window before any of it starts compounding. The creators who get this wrong tend to spend money on ads or automation tools before they've proven the community retains anyone past week one; the creators who get it right treat the first 90 days as a retention problem first and an acquisition problem second.

Once a community clears that early retention hurdle, the signals that matter (consistent growth, stable pricing, and real engagement) become visible to anyone evaluating it from the outside, not just to the operator.
