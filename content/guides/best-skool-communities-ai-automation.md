## How to Pick Between Them

None of the numbers above replace actually looking at what happens inside a community day to day, but a few things are worth weighing before you join anything.

Price is not a quality signal on its own. Some of the largest, most active groups on this list, The Founders Club, Clief Notes, ZeroOne Systems, charge nothing to join, while a $7/month or $47 one-time fee elsewhere can be either a genuine bargain or a thin funnel into a bigger upsell. What you're paying for is what happens after you're inside, not the number on the price tag.

Member count matters, but only as one signal among several. A community with 164,000 members needs active daily discussion to justify that size, and a community with 5,000 can be tighter and more responsive precisely because it's smaller. Look at whether recent posts are getting real replies, not just the headline total.

Check whether a free tier is a real product or a teaser for something else. Several of the largest communities here are free and clearly structured as an on-ramp into a paid offer, which is a legitimate model, but it helps to know which one you're joining before you sink time into it.

Tool-specific communities deserve a different evaluation than general ones. Learn Microsoft Fabric and SW Automation exist because they serve people committed to one specific platform, Microsoft Fabric or n8n, rather than AI broadly, so the right question isn't "is this the best AI community" but "does this match the exact tool or workflow I'm actually using."

Finally, check the TrustSkore before you decide anything. Growth trend, posting activity, and pricing history over time tell you more about whether a community is actually alive than any single snapshot number can.
