# How to Price a Skool Community: What the Data Actually Shows

Pricing a Skool community is usually treated as a gut-feel decision. It doesn't have to be. Enough communities now exist on the platform that a few independent analyses have looked at pricing patterns across hundreds or thousands of groups. Here's what that data actually shows, and where the honest tradeoffs are.

## The Pricing Landscape Right Now

One analysis of the public Skool leaderboard found pricing clusters into four bands: $9-$19/month covers roughly 41% of active paid Skools, the $29-$49 band covers another 36%, high-ticket communities at $79-$199/month represent 17%, and premium communities above $200/month make up the remaining 6% ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/skool-revenue-benchmarks-2026/)). A separate analysis of the top 1,000 communities found a 40/60 split between free and paid groups, with paid communities averaging $376.77 in listed price and the overall sample averaging 5,315 members ([Joe Apfelbaum](https://joeapfelbaum.substack.com/p/skool-communities-analysis-top-1000)) — a figure clearly pulled upward by a smaller number of high-ticket coaching and mastermind offers sitting alongside the much larger population of $9-$49 communities.

## The "Sweet Spot" Most Creators Land On

Across several pricing breakdowns, the range that shows up most often for general paid communities is $27-$97/month, with $47-$67/month cited specifically as the sweet spot for coaching-style communities: high enough to filter for genuinely committed members, low enough to keep cancellation friction low ([Mihael Cacic](https://www.mihaelcacic.com/pricing-analysis/skool-pricing/)). That's a useful starting range, not a guarantee, since the "right" number still depends heavily on niche.

## Price Alone Doesn't Predict Revenue

Here's the counterintuitive part: pricing tier does not reliably predict monthly recurring revenue on its own. The $29-$49 band produces close to the same median MRR as the $9-$19 band, because lower-priced communities tend to compensate with substantially higher member counts ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/skool-revenue-benchmarks-2026/)). What actually predicts MRR is the pairing of price with format, specifically whether a community runs a paid, time-boxed cohort program alongside its baseline subscription, rather than relying purely on an evergreen content drip ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/skool-revenue-benchmarks-2026/)).

At the roughly $10K MRR tier, a typical profile looks like 180-320 members paying $29-$59/month, plus a separate paid cohort running two to four times a year at a $97-$297 enrollment price. Creators fitting this pattern run about 3.2 cohorts annually at an average of 84 enrollments and a blended $167 price point, adding roughly $52K in annualized revenue on top of the subscription line ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/skool-revenue-benchmarks-2026/)).

## Free vs Low-Ticket vs Premium: The Real Tradeoffs

**Free communities** are a growth flywheel, not a revenue line. They convert almost nothing directly, but they're disproportionately common at the top of the platform, in AI and automation specifically, free communities dominate the largest groups, functioning as funnels toward a separate paid tier ([Codingoblin](https://codingoblin.com/blog/most-profitable-skool-community-niches)). Free also tends to help discoverability, since Skool's own discovery and search layer rewards growth velocity and activity, both of which are easier to generate at zero price friction ([Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/152-how-to-rank-higher-in-discovery)).

**Low-ticket communities ($9-$49/month)** need volume to work. They rely on a large top-of-funnel and tend to have lower per-member cancellation friction, but also thinner margins per member and a bigger onboarding and support burden spread across more people.

**Premium communities ($97+/month)** need far fewer members to hit meaningful revenue, but the bar for what members expect rises with the price: real curriculum, responsive admins, and visible outcomes, not just a chat feed. Thin content at a premium price is also the fastest way to spike churn, since members with real money on the line notice quickly when there's nothing new inside.

## How Churn Should Actually Shape Your Pricing Decision

Retention, not top-line price, is the number to watch. The average churn rate across Skool communities sits around 18%, with anything under 10% considered good; churn is highest in the first three months (roughly 20%) before dropping to around 10% after 90 days and further to about 2% after six months ([Skootcrm](https://www.skootcrm.com/blog/how-to-reduce-skool-churn); [Educate](https://www.skool.com/educate/average-churn-levels)). If a price point produces high month-one cancellations regardless of how strong the top-of-funnel looks, that's a signal the price and the delivered value are mismatched, not a marketing problem to throw more ads at.

## How to Actually Choose

A reasonable sequence: start free or low-ticket to prove genuine retention and real demand, then layer in a premium or cohort-based tier once you have evidence people stick around and get results. Watch churn, not just signups, as your primary health metric. And treat your price as something that should stay stable once set, since erratic pricing, discount-then-hike patterns, disappearing tiers, is itself a signal worth tracking, both for your own members' trust and for anyone evaluating your community from the outside.

That's exactly the kind of signal that's hard to see just by looking at a community's landing page, which is why growth momentum and price stability, not sticker price alone, are worth checking before you commit.
