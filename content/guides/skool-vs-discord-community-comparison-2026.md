# Skool vs Discord: Which Should You Use for Your Community in 2026?

If you're choosing a home for a community in 2026, the decision usually comes down to two platforms: Skool and Discord. They solve different problems, and picking the wrong one costs you months of migration pain later. Here's an honest, feature-by-feature comparison of the things that actually matter: pricing, discoverability, course delivery, and moderation.

## Pricing Model: Flat Fee vs Free Forever

Discord costs community owners nothing to run at any member count. There's no platform fee and no revenue share; the optional Nitro subscription at $9.99/month is a cosmetic upgrade for individual users, not a business cost ([DiscoverSkool](https://discoverskool.com/skool-vs-discord/)). That makes Discord the default choice for anyone who isn't monetizing membership directly.

Skool charges community owners directly. The Hobby plan is $9/month with a 10% transaction fee on payments processed through the platform, while the Pro plan is $99/month with a 2.9% + $0.30 fee per transaction and no separate platform cut on sales up to $899 ([Kourses](https://kourses.com/skool-pricing/); [Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/86-subscriptions-faq)). Most creators land on a simple rule of thumb: Hobby is cheaper below roughly $1,300/month in community revenue, Pro is cheaper above it ([Kourses](https://kourses.com/skool-pricing/)).

## Discoverability: Built-In Growth Engine vs Bring Your Own Audience

This is the biggest structural difference between the two. Discord servers are invisible to outsiders unless someone already has an invite link; there's no public directory or search that surfaces your server to new people. Every member has to come from your own marketing.

Skool runs a public Discovery and search layer that can send you new members without any outside promotion, especially since its 2026 overhaul introduced a trending homepage, a "Top" vs "Trending" sort, and up to 11 owner-assigned keywords per community for search matching ([Skoolprep](https://skoolprep.com/skool-discovery-update-2026); [Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/152-how-to-rank-higher-in-discovery)). With roughly 170,000 communities and more than 25 million users on the platform, that internal discovery layer is a meaningful acquisition channel Discord simply doesn't offer ([Thoughtlytics](https://www.thoughtlytics.com/newsletter/skool-growth-strategies)).

## Courses and Classroom Features

Skool was built around structured learning: step-by-step course modules, a dedicated classroom area, and a points-and-levels gamification system layered on top of the community feed ([Skoolmakers](https://skoolmakers.com/comparisons/skool-vs-discord/)). If your offer includes any kind of curriculum, cohort, or paid program, Skool's classroom tab handles it natively, with progress tracking and drip scheduling built in.

Discord has no native learning management system. Communities that teach structured content on Discord typically bolt on third-party course tools, rely on pinned channel resources, or run a separate course platform and use Discord purely as the chat layer ([Creator Stack Club](https://creatorstackclub.com/compare/discord-vs-skool)).

## Moderation: Native Simplicity vs a Mature Bot Ecosystem

Skool ships with its own AutoMod for spam and keyword filtering, alongside standard manual admin tools, but the toolset is comparatively lean ([Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/184-how-to-manage-spam-in-your-skool-community)).

Discord's native AutoMod handles keyword and spam filtering out of the box, and on top of that sits a genuinely mature third-party bot ecosystem: tools like MEE6, Carl-bot, Dyno, and VibeBot add raid protection, warning systems, and detailed moderation logging that go well beyond what either platform offers natively ([VibeBot](https://www.vibebot.gg/blog/best-discord-moderation-bots)). If you're running a large, fast-moving, real-time community, Discord's moderation depth is hard to match.

## Where Each Platform Wins

**Discord wins** when your community is free, voice- and chat-heavy, real-time by nature, or exists purely as a top-of-funnel lead magnet where cost has to stay at zero. Gaming, fan, and open-source communities fit this pattern well.

**Skool wins** when you're selling access: paid coaching, cohort programs, or any curriculum-based offer where discoverability, drip content, and built-in payments save you from stitching together three separate tools.

## Bottom Line

Neither platform is objectively better. Discord is built for conversation; Skool is built for monetized structure. Most serious paid-community operators end up running Skool as the primary paid home and, sometimes, a free Discord or Skool community as a top-of-funnel feeder. What matters more than the platform choice itself is whether the community you land in is actually active and growing, not just large on paper.

If you're evaluating Skool communities specifically, don't rely on member count alone; growth trajectory and engagement momentum tell a very different story than a static headline number.
