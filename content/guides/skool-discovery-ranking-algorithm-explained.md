# What Makes a Skool Community's Discovery Ranking Go Up (or Down)

Skool doesn't publish its ranking algorithm in full, but between its own help documentation and the 2026 overhaul the company announced publicly, there's enough disclosed to build a working picture of what actually moves a community up or down in Discovery, and what owners can realistically do about it.

## The Old System vs the New One

Skool's original discovery ranking rewarded engagement volume rather than quality, which meant low-value communities running "comment bait" tactics (posts designed purely to farm replies and likes) could out-rank genuinely better communities that just weren't gaming the metric ([Skoolprep](https://skoolprep.com/skool-discovery-update-2026)). In 2026, Skool announced a sweeping overhaul rolling out over 90 days: a new default trending homepage, new ranking algorithms, new categories, and in-platform search, explicitly built to measure quality rather than just raw activity ([Skoolprep](https://skoolprep.com/skool-discovery-update-2026)).

## What Signals Actually Move the Needle

According to Skool's own help documentation, the ranking algorithm considers three core inputs: member growth (new members joining), member activity (posts, comments, and likes), and member retention ([Skool Help Center](https://help.skool.com/article/152-how-to-rank-higher-in-discovery)). Retention is the piece that's easy to overlook: a community that grows fast but leaks members just as fast doesn't perform as well in Discovery as one that grows more slowly but keeps who it acquires.

## Top vs Trending: Two Different Games

The 2026 update introduced two distinct sorting views, and they reward different things. "Top" uses a proprietary engagement-and-quality score that tends to favor established, consistently active communities. "Trending" surfaces communities based on recent growth velocity, meaning a community launched last week with strong early momentum can appear above communities with far larger total member counts ([Skoolprep](https://skoolprep.com/skool-discovery-update-2026)). If your community is new, Trending is the lever available to you; Top is something you earn over time.

## Keywords and Search

The same update gave members the ability to search for communities directly inside Skool, and gave owners up to 11 assignable keywords per community to match against those searches ([Skoolprep](https://skoolprep.com/skool-discovery-update-2026)). This is one of the few purely mechanical levers owners control directly: choosing keywords that actually describe what members search for, rather than vague branding terms, is a straightforward way to be found by the right searches.

## What Owners Can Actually Control

**Retention, starting in week one.** Most community churn is decided almost immediately: roughly 60% of members who eventually leave a community make that decision in their very first week ([Communipass](https://communipass.com/blog/how-to-monetize-a-skool-community-30-day-roadmap-2026/)). A basic onboarding sequence (a welcome message, a progress check-in, and a problem-focused nudge) directly targets the retention input the algorithm is measuring, not just member experience in the abstract.

**Genuine engagement over engagement bait.** Because the 2026 update specifically targets comment-bait tactics, posts that exist purely to generate replies are a weaker growth strategy today than they were before. Substantive posts that generate real discussion serve both the algorithm and the actual member experience, which is the point of the change.

**Consistent posting cadence.** Communities that post regularly, rather than in bursts followed by silence, give the algorithm continuous activity signal to measure rather than spikes that look like manipulation.

**Accurate keyword selection.** Since owners can assign up to 11 keywords, treating this like basic SEO keyword research, matching what a prospective member would actually type, rather than treating it as branding real estate, is a direct, low-effort ranking lever.

## What Doesn't Help Anymore

Tactics built around inflating raw activity numbers, comment-bait prompts, engagement-pod style reciprocal liking, artificially inflated member counts through incentivized free joins with no real intent to participate, work against you under a quality-first system rather than for you. If your community's activity looks impressive in volume but thin in substance, the 2026 algorithm is specifically designed to discount that.

## The Practical Takeaway

Discovery ranking isn't a black box you have to guess at: it's built from growth, activity, and retention, weighted differently depending on whether you're being surfaced under Top or Trending, plus a keyword-matching layer you control directly. The fastest lever for a new community is Trending, driven by early growth velocity and strong week-one retention; the slower, more durable lever is Top, built from sustained, genuine engagement over time.

Because this is exactly the kind of momentum that's hard to eyeball from a landing page, it's worth checking growth and retention trends directly before deciding a community is worth your time.
